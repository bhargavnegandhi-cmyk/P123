A dollar today is not a dollar tomorrow.

Every valuation an analyst ever does — a bond, a stock, a pension liability, a mortgage — is really just this one idea applied over and over: move money through time, honestly. This module builds that skill from a single $100 deposit up to full annuity and perpetuity valuation, with a real interactive time-value calculator you can use for every problem below.

01

Interest Rates — Three Meanings, One Number

Before any formula, it helps to know what an interest rate actually represents.

Suppose you'd accept $9,500 today in place of $10,000 a year from now. The $500 gap — $500/$9,500 = 5.26% — is the interest rate, denoted r, that makes the two amounts equivalent in value. That single number can be read three different ways depending on context:

Three readings of the same rate

Required rate of return — the minimum return an investor demands to accept an investment.

Discount rate — the rate used to pull a future amount back to its value today.

Opportunity cost — what you give up by tying your money up in one place instead of the next-best alternative.

Zoom into where that rate actually comes from, and it's built out of five layers stacked on top of each other:

The anatomy of an interest rate
r = Real risk-free rate + Inflation premium + Default risk premium + Liquidity premium + Maturity premium
ComponentCompensates for
Real risk-free ratePure time preference — consuming later instead of now
Inflation premiumExpected loss of purchasing power
Default risk premiumChance the borrower doesn't pay as promised
Liquidity premiumDifficulty converting the investment to cash quickly at fair value
Maturity premiumExtra price sensitivity of longer-dated debt to rate changes

The real risk-free rate plus the inflation premium together make up the nominal risk-free rate — approximated in practice by a short-term government bill, like a 90-day US Treasury bill, which carries essentially no default or liquidity premium.

Check yourself
A bond issued by a small, thinly-traded company carries a higher interest rate than an otherwise identical bond from a large, frequently-traded issuer. The gap is best explained by:
Correct — a bond that's hard to sell quickly without moving its price carries a liquidity premium. Thin trading is the classic signal of low liquidity.

Same word, same idea: throughout this module, "interest rate," "discount rate," and "required return" are used interchangeably — context tells you which lens you're using, but the underlying number is identical.

02

Future Value of a Single Cash Flow

The engine behind every time-value problem: compounding.

Invest $100 today (PV = $100) at 5% for one year, and you'll have $105 — the original $100 plus $5 of interest. Leave it another year, and you don't just earn another $5; you earn 5% on the full $105, giving $110.25. That extra $0.25 is interest earned on interest — the phenomenon of compounding.

Future value formula
FVN = PV(1 + r)N

r and N must be defined in the same time units — an annual rate needs N in years, a monthly rate needs N in months.

Worked example — the power of compounding

$5,000,000 invested for 5 years at 7% compounded annually: FV = $5,000,000(1.07)5 = $5,000,000(1.402552) = $7,012,758.65. Stretch that same 7% out over 100 years instead, and $100 grows to roughly $13,150 — but bump the rate to 13%, and 100 years turns $100 into more than $20 million. Small differences in rate compound into enormous differences over long horizons.

Check yourself
$10,000 is invested for 3 years at 6% compounded annually. What is the future value?
Correct — FV = $10,000(1.06)³ = $10,000(1.191016) = $11,910.16. The $11,800 answer is simple (non-compounded) interest — a common trap.

03

Compounding Frequency & the Effective Annual Rate

"8% compounded monthly" and "8% compounded annually" are not the same rate — even though both say "8%."

Banks often quote a stated annual rate (rs), then compound it more than once a year. With m compounding periods per year, each period earns rs/m, and there are m×N periods total over N years:

Future value with non-annual compounding
FVN = PV(1 + rs/m)mN

More frequent compounding always produces a larger ending amount for the same stated rate — because interest starts earning its own interest sooner. Push the compounding frequency to its limit (m → ∞) and you get continuous compounding:

Continuous compounding
FVN = PV · ersN

The future value of $1 at a stated 8% annual rate, under six compounding frequencies. Notice how quickly the gains from more frequent compounding shrink — daily is already almost indistinguishable from continuous.

Because the stated rate alone doesn't tell you the true annual growth, we convert it into the effective annual rate (EAR) — the rate that, compounded just once a year, gives the same ending value:

Effective annual rate
EAR = (1 + rs/m)m − 1  (discrete compounding)
EAR = ers − 1  (continuous compounding)
Worked example — comparing two CD offers

Bank A offers 8% compounded semiannually; Bank B offers 8% compounded monthly. EAR(A) = (1.04)² − 1 = 8.16%. EAR(B) = (1 + 0.08/12)12 − 1 = 8.30%. Same stated rate, but Bank B's more frequent compounding makes it the better deal — the EAR is what makes them properly comparable.

Check yourself
A rate of 12% is compounded quarterly. What is the effective annual rate?
Correct — EAR = (1 + 0.12/4)⁴ − 1 = (1.03)⁴ − 1 = 1.1255 − 1 = 12.55%.

04

Future Value of a Series of Cash Flows

Most real cash flow streams aren't a single lump sum — they're a sequence of payments.

Vocabulary

Annuity — a finite set of level, sequential cash flows.

Ordinary annuity — first payment one period from now (t = 1).

Annuity due — first payment immediately (t = 0).

To find the future value of an ordinary annuity, in principle you'd compound each payment forward to the final date and add them up. Do that algebra once and it collapses into a single closed-form factor:

Future value of an ordinary annuity
FVN = A · [ ((1 + r)N − 1) / r ]

The bracketed term is the future value annuity factor — the future value of $1-per-period, for N periods at rate r.

Worked example — retirement contributions

€20,000 invested at the end of each year for 30 years at 9%: FV annuity factor = [(1.09)30 − 1] / 0.09 = 136.307539. FV = €20,000 × 136.307539 = €2,726,150.77.

When payments are unequal, there's no shortcut — compound each cash flow individually to the same future date, then add:

05

Present Value of a Single Cash Flow

Run the future-value formula backward, and you're discounting instead of compounding.

If $100 today grows to $105 in a year at 5%, then $100 is exactly the present value of that future $105. Solving FVN = PV(1 + r)N for PV gives the discounting formula:

Present value formula
PV = FVN (1 + r)−N

The present value factor (1+r)−N is simply the reciprocal of the future value factor.

Worked example — funding a future payment

An insurer must pay $100,000 in 6 years and can earn 8%. PV = $100,000(1.08)−6 = $100,000(0.6301696) = $63,016.96 — that's the amount to invest today.

Two intuitive relationships fall straight out of the formula:

  • Holding the discount rate fixed, a payment further in the future has a smaller present value.
  • Holding time fixed, a higher discount rate produces a smaller present value.

Non-annual compounding works exactly the same way here as it did for future value — swap in the periodic rate and the matching number of periods:

Present value with non-annual compounding
PV = FVN (1 + rs/m)−mN
Check yourself
A GIC promises C$5,000,000 in 10 years. At 6% compounded monthly, what should you invest today?
Correct — PV = C$5,000,000 × (1.005)⁻¹²⁰ = C$5,000,000 × 0.549633 = C$2,748,163.67. Note the monthly rate (0.5%) and 120 monthly periods.

Present Value of a Series of Cash Flows

What is a stream of future payments worth today? This is the single most-used calculation in valuation.

Present value of an ordinary annuity
PV = A · [ 1 − (1 + r)−N ] / r
Worked example — pricing an income stream

An asset pays €1,000 a year for 5 years, first payment in one year, discounted at 12%: PV = €1,000 × [1 − (1.12)⁻⁵]/0.12 = €1,000 × 3.604776 = €3,604.78.

An annuity due is just an ordinary annuity shifted one period earlier — the whole stream is worth (1+r) times more, since every payment is discounted one less period:

Present value of an annuity due
PV(due) = A · { [1 − (1 + r)−N] / r } · (1 + r)
Worked example — lump sum vs. annuity due retirement benefit

Choice A: $2,000,000 today. Choice B: 20 annual payments of $200,000, starting today, at 7%. Split B into "$200,000 now" plus a 19-payment ordinary annuity: PV(ordinary 19-yr annuity) = $200,000 × 10.335595 = $2,067,119.05. Add the immediate $200,000:

Total PV(B) = $200,000 + $2,067,119.05 = $2,267,119.05 — larger than Choice A's flat $2,000,000, so the annuity option wins.

When cash flows are unequal, discount and sum each one individually — the same brute-force approach as for future value, just run in reverse:

Present Value of a Perpetuity

An annuity that never stops paying — the model behind preferred stock and level consol bonds.

Let N in the ordinary annuity formula grow without bound, and the term (1+r)−N shrinks to zero — leaving a remarkably simple result:

Present value of a perpetuity
PV = A / r

Valid only for level, identical, never-ending payments, with the first payment one period from now.

Worked example — a consol bond

A British consol bond pays £100 a year forever. At a 5% required return: PV = £100 / 0.05 = £2,000.

Perpetuities don't always start paying next period. A deferred (projected) perpetuity needs an extra step: value it as of one period before its first payment, then discount that lump sum back to today.

Worked example — a perpetuity starting later

A £100 perpetuity's first payment is at t = 5. Step 1: value it at t = 4 (one period before the first payment) as an ordinary perpetuity: PV4 = £100/0.05 = £2,000. Step 2: discount that £2,000 back 4 more years to today:

PV0 = £2,000 × (1.05)⁻⁴ = £2,000 × 0.822702 = £1,645.40.

That same two-step logic gives a slick way to value a plain N-year annuity: it's the difference between two perpetuities — one starting now, one starting right after the annuity would have ended.

An annuity as the difference of two perpetuities

A 4-year, £100 ordinary annuity at 5% = Perpetuity 1 (starts t=1, worth £2,000 today) minus Perpetuity 2 (starts t=5, worth £2,000 at t=4, or £1,645.40 today). PV(annuity) = £2,000 − £1,645.40 = £354.60.

Check yourself
A perpetuity pays $40 a year forever, first payment in one year, at an 8% discount rate. What's its present value?
Correct — PV = A/r = $40/0.08 = $500.

Solving for Interest Rates, Growth Rates & Number of Periods

So far, r and N were given. In practice, they're often what you're solving for.

Rearranging FVN = PV(1 + r)N lets you solve for whichever variable is missing. Solved for the rate, this is also how you compute a growth rate — the same math, different name:

Growth rate formula
g = (FVN / PV)1/N − 1
Worked example — a five-year growth rate

Hyundai Steel's sales grew from ₩14,146.4B (2012) to ₩19,166.0B (2017) — a 5-year span. g = (19,166.0/14,146.4)1/5 − 1 = (1.354832)0.2 − 1 ≈ 6.26% per year. Over the same period, net profit actually declined, from ₩796.4B to ₩727.5B: g = (727.5/796.4)1/5 − 1 ≈ −1.79% per year — a negative compound growth rate.

Solve instead for N, and you get the number of periods it takes an investment to reach a target value:

Solving for N
N = ln(FV/PV) / ln(1 + r)
Worked example — doubling time, and the Rule of 72

How long does €10,000,000 take to double at 7%? N = ln(2)/ln(1.07) = 10.24 years.

A quick mental shortcut, the Rule of 72, approximates this without logarithms: divide 72 by the interest rate (as a whole number). Here, 72/7 ≈ 10.3 years — remarkably close to the exact 10.24.

Check yourself
An investment grows from $50,000 to $80,000 over 6 years. What is the annual compound growth rate?
Correct — g = (80,000/50,000)^(1/6) − 1 = (1.6)^0.1667 − 1 ≈ 8.14%. The 60% figure is total growth, not annualized.

Solving for the Size of Annuity Payments

Mortgages, auto loans, and retirement drawdowns all boil down to one question: what fixed payment matches a given present value?

Rearranging the ordinary annuity present value formula for A gives the payment size directly:

Solving for the annuity payment
A = PV / { [1 − (1 + r)−N] / r }
Worked example — a fixed-rate mortgage

A $120,000 house, $20,000 down, financed with a $100,000, 30-year mortgage at 8% compounded monthly (r = 0.08/12 = 0.006667, N = 360 months): the present value annuity factor works out to about 136.283494, so A = $100,000 / 136.283494 ≈ $733.76 per month.

The same rearrangement works for the future-value annuity factor when you're solving for a savings contribution needed to hit a target future value, rather than a loan payment matching a present value.

Check yourself
You need a present value of $50,000 to be fully paid off by an ordinary annuity of N=10 annual payments at 6%. The PV annuity factor is 7.360087. What's the required payment?
Correct — A = PV / annuity factor = $50,000 / 7.360087 ≈ $6,793.40.

Present & Future Value Equivalence — the Additivity Principle

The one rule that ties everything in this module together.

The cash flow additivity principle

Amounts of money indexed at the same point in time are additive. Put a lump sum, an annuity, and a future value all on the same time index, and you can add, subtract, and substitute freely between them.

This isn't just a bookkeeping convenience — a lump sum, an annuity, and its future value are all literally the same amount of money, just viewed from different dates. A $4,329.48 deposit today at 5%, for instance, can fund exactly five $1,000 withdrawals, one at the end of each of the next five years, with the account hitting zero right after the last withdrawal:

How a $4,329.48 lump sum funds a 5-year, $1,000 annuity at 5%
YearStart BalanceGrows toWithdrawalEnd Balance
1$4,329.48$4,545.95$1,000$3,545.95
2$3,545.95$3,723.25$1,000$2,723.25
3$2,723.25$2,859.41$1,000$1,859.41
4$1,859.41$1,952.38$1,000$952.38
5$952.38$1,000.00$1,000$0.00

The same idea also lets you combine two cash flow series before valuing them, instead of valuing them separately and adding the results — both routes always agree.

Worked example — combining two series

Series A pays $100 at t=1 and t=2. Series B pays $200 at t=1 and t=2. At 2% interest:

FV(A) = $100(1.02) + $100 = $202. FV(B) = $200(1.02) + $200 = $404. FV(A) + FV(B) = $606. Now combine the series first — $300 at t=1 and t=2 — and value that instead: $300(1.02) + $300 = $606. Same answer either way, exactly as the additivity principle guarantees.

Check yourself
The cash flow additivity principle allows you to add or subtract two cash flows only if:
Correct — money can only be added or subtracted once it's expressed as of the same date; $100 today and $100 in five years are not directly comparable until one is moved to match the other's date.
