Turn a pile of numbers into an insight.

Part 1 of this module covers the first half of the journey: classifying data, organizing it, counting it, and charting it — using one real, worked dataset after another, from a portfolio of 1,000 stocks to five years of index returns. Part 2 picks up from here and covers boiling that organized data down into honest summary numbers.

01

Data Types

Before you can choose the right chart or the right statistic, you need to know what kind of data you're holding.

The same underlying fact — "this company is in the energy sector" — behaves very differently in a spreadsheet than "this company's EPS is $7.50." Analysts sort data along three independent axes, and each one changes what you're allowed to do with the numbers.

Axis 1 — Numerical vs. Categorical

Numerical (quantitative) data are measured or counted amounts you can do arithmetic on. They split further into continuous (can take any value in a range — a stock's price return) and discrete (counted, finite values — the number of coupon payments left on a bond).

Categorical (qualitative) data are labels describing a quality. Nominal categories have no natural order (GICS sector: Energy, Financials…). Ordinal categories can be ranked, but the gaps between ranks aren't necessarily equal (a 5-star Morningstar rating, or a bond's letter credit rating).

The rule of thumb that resolves most edge cases: meaningful arithmetic can be performed on numerical data but not on categorical data — even when categories are coded as numbers. GICS sector code 10 (Energy) isn't "less than" sector code 60 (Real Estate); the numbers are just labels.

Axis 2 — Cross-Sectional vs. Time-Series vs. Panel

Cross-sectional data: one variable, many units, one point in time — January inflation for every country in the Eurozone.

Time-series data: one variable, one unit, many points in time — a single stock's daily closing price over a month.

Panel data: a mix of both — quarterly EPS for three different companies, across four quarters. Read down a column and it's a time series for one company; read across a row and it's a cross-section of all three companies in one quarter.

Axis 3 — Structured vs. Unstructured

Structured data are pre-organized into rows and columns — market prices, financial-statement line items. Unstructured data don't follow a fixed layout — earnings-call transcripts, news articles, satellite imagery, social media posts. Unstructured data often carry unique insight, but a model can't consume them directly; they have to be transformed into structured form first.

Correct — ratings can be ranked from best to worst credit quality, but the "distance" between AAA and AA isn't necessarily the same as between B and CCC. That's ordinal data.

02

Organizing Data for Quantitative Analysis

Raw data pulled from a website or a filing almost never arrives ready to analyze. Two simple structures fix that.

One-dimensional array

A single column of values for one variable, most often in time order. New observations simply extend the array — nothing else has to change.

Example — ABC Inc.'s first 10 trading days

A one-dimensional array of daily closing prices after ABC's IPO:

Day12345678910
Price ($)57.2158.2658.6456.1954.7854.2656.8854.7452.4250.14
Two-dimensional rectangular array (a "data table")

Columns represent variables, rows represent observations in time order — like an Excel sheet. This is the standard shape models and computers expect.

Worked example — building a data table

ABC Inc.'s raw quarterly filings arrive grouped awkwardly by fiscal year. Reorganized into a proper data table (one row per quarter, one column per metric):

Note DPS = 0 for the first five quarters — ABC simply hadn't started paying dividends yet, so zero is the correct, meaningful value. Contrast that with a genuinely missing data point (say, a data-vendor outage), which should never be silently filled with zero — that would misrepresent the truth. Better options are the latest available value or an interpolated estimate.

03

Summarizing Data Using Frequency Distributions

Count how often things happen, and a wall of numbers turns into a story.

A frequency distribution (also called a one-way table) tallies observations into groups. For categorical data it's simple: count occurrences of each unique value — the number of stocks in each GICS sector, for instance. For numerical data, you first need to build non-overlapping bins.

The seven-step procedure for numerical data

1. Sort the data ascending. 2. Compute Range = Max − Min. 3. Choose the number of bins, k. 4. Bin width = Range / k (round up). 5. Build each bin by successively adding the bin width. 6. Count observations in each bin (last bin includes the maximum). 7. Tabulate.

Worked example — 18 countries' equity index returns

Range = 9.9% − 5.5% = 4.4%. Choosing k = 5 bins gives a bin width of 4.4/5 = 0.88%, rounded up to 1.0%. Starting at the nearest whole number below the minimum (5.0%):

BinAbsolute Freq.Relative Freq.Cumulative Rel. Freq.
5.0 – 6.0%15.56%5.56%
6.0 – 7.0%422.22%27.78%
7.0 – 8.0%633.33%61.11%
8.0 – 9.0%422.22%83.33%
9.0 – 10.0%316.67%100.00%

A third of the countries land in the 7–8% bin — the modal bin. Reading down the cumulative column, you instantly see that roughly 61% of countries returned 8% or less.

Relative frequency = a bin's count ÷ total observations — it lets you compare datasets of very different sizes. Cumulative frequency (absolute or relative) running-totals as you move through the bins, so the last bin's cumulative relative frequency is always 100%.

Categorical frequency distributions work the same way

No binning needed — just count. Among a 1,000-stock portfolio, if Health Care holds 435 stocks, its relative frequency is 435/1,000 = 43.5%. Line every sector up from most to least common, and you have a categorical frequency distribution — exactly the data behind the bar chart and tree-map you'll build later in this module.

Check yourself
A dataset of 200 observations has a minimum of 10 and a maximum of 90. If you build a frequency distribution with k = 8 bins, what is the bin width?
Correct — Range = 90 − 10 = 80. Bin width = 80 / 8 = 10 exactly.

04

Summarizing Data Using a Contingency Table

A frequency distribution handles one variable. A contingency table handles two, at the same time.

A contingency table (or two-way table) lists one variable's categories as rows and another's as columns. An R-row, C-column table is called an "R × C table." The counts inside the cells are joint frequencies; the row and column totals are marginal frequencies.

Worked example — a 1,000-stock portfolio by sector & market cap
SectorSmallMidLargeTotal
Communication Svcs553520110
Consumer Staples503030110
Energy1759520290
Health Care27510555435
Utilities20251055
Total5752901351,000

275 small-cap health care stocks is the single largest joint frequency in the table — over a quarter of the entire portfolio in one cell. Summing across a row (435 total health care stocks) or down a column (575 total small-cap stocks) gives the marginal frequencies — literally the frequency distribution for that one variable alone.

The same joint frequencies can be expressed as a percentage three different ways — and each answers a genuinely different question. Toggle between them below, using the exact same portfolio data:

Every cell as a share of the full 1,000-stock portfolio.

Reading the three views

% of overall total answers "how much of my whole portfolio is this?" (27.5% is small-cap health care). % of row total answers "within this sector, how is it split by size?" (63.2% of health care holdings are small-cap). % of column total answers "within this cap size, which sectors dominate?" (47.8% of all small-cap holdings are health care). Same 20 numbers, three different stories.

A special case — the confusion matrix

When one variable is a model's prediction and the other is the actual outcome, a 2×2 contingency table is called a confusion matrix. For a model predicting bond defaults across 2,000 non-investment-grade bonds:

Predicted \ ActualDefaultNo DefaultTotal
Default30040340
No Default101,6501,660
Total3101,6902,000

The model correctly flags 300 real defaults and 1,650 real non-defaults (the diagonal), but wrongly predicts default 40 times and wrongly predicts safety 10 times (off-diagonal). A contingency table like this is also how analysts informally check whether two categorical variables seem related at all — here, sector and market cap are clearly not independent, given how heavily small-cap health care and energy dominate the table.

Check yourself
In the portfolio contingency table above, what's the marginal frequency of energy-sector stocks?
Correct — sum across the Energy row: 175 + 95 + 20 = 290.

Reading direction matters. Dividing by the overall total tells you "% of the whole portfolio." Dividing by a row total tells you "within this sector, how is it split by cap size?" Dividing by a column total tells you "within this cap size, how is it split by sector?" Same table, three different questions.

Histograms & Frequency Polygons

The chart version of a frequency distribution table — and the fastest way to see a distribution's shape, center, and spread at a glance.

A histogram uses bar height to show each bin's frequency. Bins sit on the x-axis; frequency (absolute or relative) sits on the y-axis. Below is the actual histogram for five years of daily returns (1,258 observations) on a fictitious EAA Equity Index, using 1% bins.

Hover a bar for its exact count. Notice the shape: roughly bell-shaped, but taller on the positive side — consistent with 54% of daily returns being positive.

A frequency polygon plots each bin's midpoint against its frequency and connects the dots with straight lines — the same information as a histogram, but as an outline instead of bars, which makes it easy to overlay several distributions on one chart for comparison.

A close cousin, the cumulative frequency distribution chart, plots cumulative (not per-bin) frequency against each bin's upper limit. Its slope tells you where observations are dense: a steep slope means a lot of observations packed into that stretch, a flat slope means very few.

Reading the shape

The modal bin here — 0.0% to 1.0% — holds 555 of the 1,258 observations (44%). Because 81.5% of all daily returns fall within just the two central bins (−1% to 1%), the cumulative-frequency curve is nearly flat at the extremes and very steep through the middle — a classic signature of returns clustering tightly around a small positive average.

Check yourself
On a cumulative relative frequency chart, a very steep section of the curve tells you that region has:
Correct — a steep slope means the cumulative count is climbing fast, i.e., many observations are packed into that stretch of bins.
Reading a histogram

Tallest bar = modal bin. Symmetric shape around the tallest bar = roughly balanced distribution. Bars trailing off further on one side than the other = skewed distribution — a concept Part 2 of this module returns to when comparing the mean and median.

Line Charts & Bubble Line Charts

How a variable changes over time — and how to add a second and third dimension to that same picture.

A line chart plots ordered observations and connects them — the natural choice for time series. Below, ABC Inc.'s first 10 days of trading (the same one-dimensional array from Module 02):

The price peaks on Day 3, drifts lower, has a brief Day 7 recovery, then falls steeply toward $50 by Day 10 — a pattern the raw data table alone doesn't convey nearly as quickly.

Add a second data series (say, ABC's sector index) with its own color and often its own secondary y-axis, and a line chart becomes a comparison tool — did the stock move for company-specific reasons, or because the whole sector moved?

Sometimes there's a third variable worth showing on the same chart. A bubble line chart replaces each line-chart marker with a circular bubble sized by a third variable — and often colored to encode a fourth piece of information.

ABC Inc.'s eight quarters of revenue (the line) with bubble size representing the magnitude of EPS that quarter, colored green for a profit and red for a loss. Q3–Q4 of Year 1 show shrinking revenue and a swing into large losses — a bubble line chart shows both stories in a single glance that two separate charts couldn't deliver as immediately.

Check yourself
You want one chart showing a company's quarterly revenue trend over time, while also indicating whether each quarter's earnings per share was a profit or a loss and by roughly how much. The best fit is a:
Correct — a bubble line chart adds a third dimension (bubble size) and often a fourth (color) on top of a standard two-dimensional line chart, exactly fitting this need.

Don't overload it: a bubble line chart is powerful precisely because it's still reading as one coherent picture. Push past three or four dimensions crammed into one chart and it stops being readable — at that point, separate charts (or a scatter plot matrix, next) usually communicate better.

07

Scatter Plots & Scatter Plot Matrices

Does one variable move with another? A scatter plot is built to answer exactly that question.

A scatter plot puts one numerical variable on each axis and plots a dot per observation. A tight, upward-sloping cloud of points signals a strong positive relationship; a formless cloud signals no relationship at all.

IT sector returns cluster tightly along a rising line with the broad market — a strong positive relationship, with one flagged outlier month.

Reading a scatter plot

Three things to look for: (1) direction — does the cloud slope up, down, or go nowhere? (2) tightness — how closely do points hug an imaginary line (tighter = stronger relationship)? (3) outliers — any points far from the main cloud, worth investigating separately.

Need to inspect pairwise relationships among more than two variables at once? A scatter plot matrix arranges every pairwise scatter plot into a grid — variable i's row against variable j's column — with each variable's own histogram often placed along the diagonal where a self-comparison would otherwise sit.

Where this shows up in practice

Before building a multi-factor model, analysts often scan a scatter plot matrix of every candidate factor against every other — it's a fast way to spot which pairs of factors are suspiciously correlated with each other (a warning sign for multicollinearity) before any formal statistics are run.

Check yourself
A scatter plot of two variables shows points scattered with no discernible pattern across the whole chart. This suggests:
Correct — a formless cloud of points is the visual signature of little or no linear relationship.

Scatter plot matrices scale quickly: with v variables, a full matrix has v² panels (v(v−1) off-diagonal comparisons plus v histograms) — useful up to maybe 6–8 variables before it becomes too dense to read at a glance.

Bar Charts

The default tool for comparing categories — with two ways to handle a second categorical variable.

A bar chart compares categories using bar length. Below: the same 1,000-stock portfolio's marginal sector frequencies from Module 04, this time drawn from a different, slightly more granular 479-stock sample so you can see the full sector breakdown.

With two categorical variables, a plain bar chart isn't enough. A grouped (clustered) bar chart places sub-group bars side by side within each category; a stacked bar chart instead piles the sub-groups on top of each other so the total bar height shows the marginal frequency directly. Tree-Maps & Word Clouds

Two more ways to visualize categories — one built for relative size, the other built for unstructured text.

A tree-map uses rectangle area (not bar length) to represent magnitude — useful when you want the eye to compare relative size at a glance rather than read precise values.

Same sector data as the bar chart in the previous module — Health Care's rectangle visibly dominates because it holds 43.5% of the portfolio.

Caveat: tree-maps become hard to read once you nest more than about three levels of hierarchy (e.g., sector → market cap → individual stock).

A word cloud sizes words by how often they appear in a body of text — a way to visualize unstructured text data. Below, a stylized word cloud built from a company's quarterly filing, sized by frequency (green tends to mark growth/positive terms, red marks cost/negative terms):

Heat Maps & a Guide to Choosing Charts

Color as data — and how to decide which of these seven chart types actually fits your question.

A heat map represents a table of values using a color spectrum instead of (or alongside) the raw numbers — darker or more saturated cells mean larger values. Below, the same sector-by-cap portfolio data, but this time as counts of stocks in an independent, more granular sample:

Heat maps are also a standard way to visualize a correlation matrix — the strength of the linear relationship between every pair of variables in a dataset, all in one glance.

Choosing among chart types

Start from your purpose. Exploring or presenting a distribution? → histogram, frequency polygon, or cumulative distribution chart for numerical data; word cloud for unstructured text. Exploring a relationship between two variables? → scatter plot (or a scatter matrix for several). Making a comparison among categories? → bar chart, tree-map, or heat map. Comparing something over time? → line chart (or a bubble line chart for a third dimension).

Four ways charts mislead
  • Wrong chart type for the question — e.g., two separate line charts instead of a scatter plot to inspect correlation.
  • Truncated axis — a y-axis that doesn't start at zero exaggerates differences in bar height.
  • Inconsistent scales across charts being compared side by side.
  • Cherry-picked time window that flatters or exaggerates a trend.
Check yourself
A vertical bar chart shows five years of sales; the first four bars look nearly identical in height, and the fifth looks roughly three times as tall. Before concluding sales tripled in year five, what should you check first?
Correct — bar height is only proportional to value when the y-axis starts at zero. A truncated axis can make a modest change look dramatic.
