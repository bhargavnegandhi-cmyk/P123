Module 00 · Before Module 0

Every number here is a real thing, not a symbol to memorize.

This module doesn't assume a strong school background — it assumes you're willing to look at numbers as things that describe the real world (money, weight, distance, time), and rebuilds arithmetic from that ground up. If a topic feels too slow, skip ahead; if a later module in this toolkit ever feels shaky, this is the place to come back to.

How to study this — not by memorizing it

If school ever taught you math as steps to repeat rather than ideas to understand, that's not a personal failing — it's how many classrooms, in many countries, are forced to teach when time and resources are short. It just means a few habits are worth rebuilding on purpose:

  • Don't memorize a formula as symbols. Memorize what each symbol stands for in real life. "PRT/100" means nothing on its own — "principal × rate × time" describes an actual pile of money growing.
  • Before hunting for a formula, restate the question in plain words. "What number, added to itself twice, gives 30?" is a math problem even before you write a single symbol.
  • Use every calculator in this toolkit to break things on purpose. Change one input, watch what happens to the output. That's how real intuition gets built — repetition of the same worked example never quite does it.
  • Getting an answer wrong while exploring is not failure. If exams punished mistakes, that doesn't mean mistakes are dangerous here. They're just data.
01

Numbers & the Four Operations

Addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division — what each one is actually doing, not just how to execute it.

What each operation really means

Addition (+): combining two amounts into one total. 3 apples + 5 apples = 8 apples in one basket.

Subtraction (−): taking an amount away, or finding the gap between two amounts. If you have 8 apples and eat 3, subtraction tells you 5 remain.

Multiplication (×): repeated addition of the same amount. 4 × 3 means "four groups of three," or equivalently "three groups of four" — either way, 12.

Division (÷): splitting a total into equal groups, or asking how many times one amount fits into another. 12 ÷ 4 asks "if I split 12 into 4 equal groups, how big is each group?" (3) — or equally, "how many groups of 4 fit inside 12?" (also 3).

Outside example — a market stall

A vendor sells mangoes at ₹15 each. Selling 8 mangoes: 15 × 8 = ₹120 — eight groups of fifteen rupees, added together. If a customer pays with a ₹200 note, subtraction finds the change: 200 − 120 = ₹80. Every rupee transaction you've ever handled was already this arithmetic — the symbols just formalize what your hands already know how to do.

You may already know this — the multiplication table trick

Many schools teach multiplication tables purely by chanting them — "seven eights are fifty-six" — without ever showing why. Here's the why: 7 × 8 means seven groups of eight (or eight groups of seven), which is exactly 8+8+8+8+8+8+8 added seven times. The table isn't a separate fact to memorize for every pair of numbers — it's a shortcut for repeated addition, and you can always rebuild any forgotten entry by simply adding.

Check yourself
What does 6 × 4 actually represent?
Correct — 6 × 4 = 4+4+4+4+4+4 = 24, six groups of four added together (or equivalently, four groups of six).

Negative Numbers & the Number Line

A negative number isn't a strange abstract idea — it's just "owing" instead of "having."

Picture a horizontal line with 0 in the middle. Positive numbers sit to the right; negative numbers sit to the left, the same distance from zero, just mirrored. A negative number represents a deficit, a debt, a temperature below freezing, or a step backward — anything measured "in the opposite direction" from a positive amount.

The two rules that cause the most confusion

Subtracting a negative flips to adding: 5 − (−3) = 5 + 3 = 8. Taking away a debt is the same as gaining that amount.

Multiplying two negatives gives a positive: (−4) × (−3) = 12. "Removing a debt, three times" leaves you better off, not worse — the two negatives cancel.

Outside example — a bank balance

Your account shows −₹500 (you're overdrawn by ₹500). A friend repays a ₹500 debt they owed you, which you had recorded as −(−500) being removed from what you're owed... more simply: if a ₹700 deposit arrives, your new balance is −500 + 700 = ₹200. The negative number tracked the deficit perfectly the whole way through — no separate rules needed for "money you owe" versus "money you have," just one number line.

You may already know this — "minus of minus is plus"

Most schools teach this as a chantable rule: "minus × minus = plus." It's correct — but here's why: think of a negative sign as "the opposite of." The opposite of the opposite of a number is the number itself. −(−5) flips 5's direction twice, landing right back on positive 5.

Check yourself
A submarine is at −120 meters (120m below sea level) and rises 45 meters. What is its new depth?
Correct — rising is adding toward zero: −120 + 45 = −75 meters, still below sea level but closer to the surface.

Fractions, Decimals & Percentages

Three different costumes for the exact same idea: a part of a whole. Nearly every number in the rest of this toolkit wears one of these three.

Cut a pizza into 4 equal slices and eat 1. You've eaten ¼ of the pizza (a fraction), which is exactly 0.25 of the pizza (a decimal), which is exactly 25% of the pizza (a percentage — "per hundred"). All three numbers describe the identical amount of pizza; they're just three different notations for the same part-of-a-whole idea.

Converting between the three

Fraction → decimal: divide the top number by the bottom number. 3/4 → 3 ÷ 4 = 0.75.

Decimal → percentage: multiply by 100 (shift the decimal point two places right). 0.75 → 75%.

Percentage → decimal: divide by 100 (shift the decimal point two places left). 75% → 0.75.

You may already know this — the "find 10%, then adjust" trick

A very common shortcut for finding 15% of a number: find 10% (just move the decimal point one place left), then add half of that again. For 15% of 240: 10% of 240 = 24, half of 24 is 12, so 15% = 24 + 12 = 36.

Why it works: 15% is exactly 10% + 5%, and 5% is exactly half of 10%. The trick isn't a special case — it's ordinary percentage arithmetic, broken into two easier pieces and added back together. Once you see that, you can build a shortcut for any percentage the same way — 20% is double 10%; 30% is triple 10%; 12% is 10% plus a fifth of 10%.

Why this matters immediately — every rate in this toolkit

An interest rate of 8%, a P/E ratio's earnings yield, a probability of 0.35, a confidence level of 95% — every single one is a percentage or decimal wearing different clothes for a different context. There is no separate "finance math" here; it's this same conversion, applied over and over to money, chances, and proportions instead of pizza.

Check yourself
What is 3/8 written as a percentage?
Correct — 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375, and 0.375 × 100 = 37.5%.

Order of Operations — BODMAS

When a calculation has several operations mixed together, everyone needs to agree on which one happens first — otherwise the same expression gives different answers to different people.

2 + 3 × 4 — is that (2+3)×4 = 20, or 2+(3×4) = 14? Without an agreed order, both readings are equally reasonable, and that's a real problem. BODMAS (sometimes taught elsewhere as PEMDAS) is that agreement — the fixed order every calculation follows, everywhere, without exception.

BODMAS
Brackets — solve inside ( ) first
Orders — powers and roots (like x² or √x) next
Division and Multiplication — left to right, same priority
Addition and Subtraction — left to right, same priority

So 2 + 3 × 4 must be read as 2 + (3×4), since multiplication outranks addition: 2 + 12 = 14. The answer 20 would only be correct if the expression had actually been written (2+3) × 4, with explicit brackets forcing the addition to happen first.

Worked example — a longer expression

Evaluate: 5 + (8 − 3) × 2²

Brackets first: (8−3) = 5, giving 5 + 5 × 2². Orders next: 2² = 4, giving 5 + 5 × 4. Multiplication: 5 × 4 = 20, giving 5 + 20. Addition last: 5 + 20 = 25.

You may already know this — as "BODMAS" or "PEMDAS"

Indian schools typically teach this as BODMAS; American schools typically teach it as PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction). They're the identical rule, just packaged with different starting letters for memorization — brackets and parentheses are the same thing, orders and exponents are the same thing. If you learned one acronym, you already know the other.

A common trap: Division and Multiplication are not "D always before M" — they share equal priority and are done strictly left to right, whichever comes first in the expression. Same for Addition and Subtraction. 12 ÷ 3 × 2 is (12÷3)×2 = 8, not 12÷(3×2) = 2.

Check yourself
Evaluate: 10 − 2 × 3 + 1
Correct — multiplication first: 2×3=6, giving 10−6+1. Then left to right: 10−6=4, then 4+1=5.

Ratios & Proportions

A ratio compares two amounts. A proportion says two ratios are equal — and that single idea solves an enormous range of everyday problems.

If a recipe needs 2 cups of flour for every 1 cup of sugar, the ratio of flour to sugar is 2:1. Double the recipe, and you need 4 cups of flour to 2 cups of sugar — a different pair of numbers, but the same ratio, because 4:2 simplifies back down to 2:1. A proportion is exactly this statement: two ratios, equal to each other, even though the actual numbers differ.

Solving a proportion
a/b = c/d   →   a × d = b × c

Cross-multiply: the product of one ratio's top-and-bottom-opposite equals the other's.

You may already know this — the unitary method

Many schools teach ratio word problems through the "unitary method": first find the value of one unit, then scale up. "If 5 pens cost ₹60, what do 8 pens cost?" → first find 1 pen: 60÷5 = ₹12. Then find 8 pens: 12×8 = ₹96.

That's not a separate technique from proportions — it's the exact same relationship, solved one specific way. As a proportion: 5/60 = 8/x, and cross-multiplying gives 5x = 480, so x = 96. Same answer, same underlying idea, just reached by a different path. Once both feel natural, you can pick whichever is faster for a given problem.

Where this shows up ahead — weighted averages

A portfolio that's 70% stocks and 30% bonds is a ratio, 70:30, simplified from whatever the actual dollar amounts are. The Summarizing Data module's weighted mean formula is built entirely on this idea — each component's proportion of the whole determines how much it should count.

Check yourself
If 3 workers can complete a job in 12 days, using proportional reasoning, roughly how long would 6 workers take (assuming equal work rates)?
Correct — doubling the workers halves the time needed, since the total amount of work stays the same: 6 days.

Turning Words Into Math

The single skill rote learning skips most often — and the one this whole toolkit depends on constantly.

Textbook drills often repeat the same phrasing so many times that the phrasing itself becomes the memorized cue — "of" means multiply, "per" means divide. That works right up until a question is worded differently, and the whole strategy collapses. The actual skill underneath is simpler and far more durable: read slowly, identify what's known, identify what's asked, and build the relationship between them in plain language before reaching for any symbol.

A few phrases and what they usually mean

"X% of Y" → (X/100) × Y

"how much more/less" → subtraction

"for every… / per…" → a ratio or rate

"total / altogether / combined" → addition

But treat that list as a starting hint, not a rule to trust blindly — the real skill is checking whether the sentence actually means that here. "20% more than last year's ₹50,000" isn't just 20% of 50,000; it's 50,000 plus 20% of 50,000. Reading comprehension always outranks keyword-spotting.

The same underlying problem, phrased four different ways

All four of these ask exactly the same question — notice how differently they're worded:

1. "A shirt costs ₹800. During a sale, the price drops by 25%. What's the new price?"

2. "A shirt originally priced at ₹800 is discounted 25%. Find the sale price."

3. "After a 25% reduction, what does a ₹800 shirt cost?"

4. "A ₹800 shirt now sells for only 75% of its original price. What's the new price?"

All four: find 25% of 800 (= 200), then subtract from 800: ₹600. Or, spotting the shortcut in phrasing 4 directly: 75% of 800 = ₹600 in one step. Different sentences, identical arithmetic — training yourself to notice that sameness is the actual goal of this section.

Check yourself
"A company's revenue grew from ₹2,00,000 to ₹2,50,000." What single operation finds the percentage increase?
Correct — percentage increase is always the change divided by the original (starting) value: (250,000−200,000)/200,000 = 50,000/200,000 = 25%.

A habit worth building: after solving any word problem, say the answer back in a full sentence — "the shirt now costs ₹600" — and check it feels reasonable. A sale price higher than the original, or a percentage increase over 1000% from a modest change, is usually a sign a step got flipped somewhere.
