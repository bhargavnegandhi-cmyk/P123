01

Random Variables, Outcomes, and Events

The three words underneath every probability sentence you'll ever read.

Roll an ordinary six-sided die. Before it lands, you don't know what number will show — that uncertainty is the whole point. In probability language:

Definition

Random variable — a quantity whose future value is uncertain. "The number on the die" is a random variable. So is "tomorrow's high temperature" or "next quarter's earnings per share."

Outcome — one possible value the random variable can take. Rolling a 4 is an outcome. A high of 31°C is an outcome.

Event — a specified set of outcomes, one or more. "Rolling an even number" is an event built from three outcomes: {2, 4, 6}. An event can also be a single outcome, like "rolling exactly a 4."

Notice the hierarchy: a random variable can land on many outcomes, and an event is just a bucket we've drawn around some of those outcomes because we care about them together.

Outside example — weather

The random variable is tomorrow's rainfall. One outcome is "12mm of rain." The event "it rains" bundles together every outcome above 0mm — an infinite number of individual outcomes, all swept into one event because that's the question you actually care about.

Finance example

A portfolio manager cares about the random variable portfolio return. A single outcome might be "exactly 10%." But the manager's real concern is usually an event: "the portfolio earns less than 10%," which bundles every return outcome from −100% up to (but not including) 10%.

02

The Two Rules Every Probability Must Obey

Plus two words that describe how events relate to each other: mutually exclusive and exhaustive.

Whatever method you use to come up with a probability — and Module 03 covers three different methods — the number you land on has to satisfy two non-negotiable rules.

The two defining properties

1. For any event E: 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1. A probability can never be negative, and it can never exceed 1. Impossible things score 0; sure things score 1.

2. If you list out events that are mutually exclusive and exhaustive, their probabilities must add up to exactly 1.

Those two italicized words carry a lot of weight, so let's pin them down with cards instead of formulas:

Mutually exclusive

Only one can happen at a time. Drawing a card that is a heart and drawing a card that is a spade are mutually exclusive — no card is both.

Exhaustive

Together, they cover every possibility with nothing left out. "Heart, spade, club, or diamond" is exhaustive — every card belongs to exactly one suit.

Put both properties together on a die: the events {1}, {2}, {3}, {4}, {5}, {6} are mutually exclusive (a roll can't be both a 2 and a 5) and exhaustive (every roll is one of them). So P(1)+P(2)+P(3)+P(4)+P(5)+P(6) must equal 1 — and since a fair die treats each face equally, each gets 1/6.

Finance example

Define A = "the portfolio earns exactly 10%," B = "the portfolio earns below 10%," C = "the portfolio earns above 10%." A, B, and C are mutually exclusive (a return can't be in two of these buckets) and exhaustive (every possible return falls into one of them). So however you estimate them, P(A) + P(B) + P(C) = 1 — always.

Check yourself
"A stock's return is positive" and "a stock's return is above 5%" — are these two events mutually exclusive?
Correct. A return of, say, 8% is both "positive" and "above 5%" simultaneously, so the two events overlap — they are not mutually exclusive.

03

Three Ways to Arrive at a Probability

Empirical, subjective, and a priori — the source of a number changes how much you should trust it.

Where do these numbers even come from? There are three broad approaches, and analysts should always know which one they're using.

Empirical probability

Based on historical frequency — you counted what actually happened. Flip a coin 200 times, get 104 heads, and the empirical probability of heads is 104/200 = 0.52. Weakness: it assumes the future behaves like the past, and it's unreliable for rare events you have little history on.

Subjective probability

A personal judgment call, not backed by a formal count or formula. "I think there's a 70% chance my flight is delayed today, based on the storm clouds I can see" is subjective. It's informed, but it's still a belief, not a measurement.

A priori probability

Derived by pure logical reasoning, before observing anything. A fair coin has exactly two equally likely sides, so P(heads) = 1/2 — you reasoned your way there without flipping it once. a priori + empirical = "objective" because, unlike subjective probability, they don't vary from person to person.

Outside example — one question, three answers

"What's the probability of drawing exactly two heads in five coin flips?" Empirical: run the experiment 100 times, get two heads 33 times → 0.33. Subjective: you guess it's "somewhere around a third" → 0.35, based on gut feel. A priori: apply the binomial formula for five fair-coin flips → 0.3125, the mathematically exact answer. All three are legitimate probabilities — they just carry different amounts of rigor.

Finance example

"51 of the 60 stocks in this index pay a dividend, so P(dividend-paying) = 51/60 = 0.85" is empirical. A credit analyst saying "I believe there's a 15% chance this bond defaults, based on my read of management" is subjective. There's rarely a clean a priori probability in investing, because market outcomes aren't symmetric like a coin or die — which is exactly why empirical and subjective estimates matter so much in practice.

Check yourself
A trader says, "Based on the last 250 trading days, this stock closed higher 140 times, so I estimate a 56% chance it closes higher tomorrow." This is:
Right — it's built from a historical frequency count (140 out of 250 days), which is the definition of empirical probability.

Empirical and a priori probabilities are grouped together as objective — two careful people using the same method land on the same number. Subjective probability can differ from person to person, which is why it's kept in its own category.

04

Odds: Probability's Other Language

Sportsbooks, racetracks, and CFA exam questions all speak in "3 to 1," not "0.75." Here's the translation.

Odds and probability describe the same underlying likelihood, just packaged differently. The odds for an event compare the probability it happens to the probability it doesn't.

Formulas
Odds for E = P(E) / [1 − P(E)]
Odds against E = [1 − P(E)] / P(E)

Given "odds for E = a to b": P(E) = a / (a + b)
Given "odds against E = a to b": P(E) = b / (a + b)

Read that carefully — odds against flip the a and b when you convert back to probability. That reversal trips people up more than any other part of this topic.

Outside example — a horse race

A bookmaker quotes a horse at "4 to 1 against." That means the implied probability of winning is 1/(4+1) = 0.20 — a 20% chance. If instead the horse were quoted "4 to 1 for," the implied probability would flip to 4/(4+1) = 0.80. Same numbers, opposite meaning, depending on the word "for" versus "against."

Worked example — passing a course

A colleague has a 0.40 probability of passing an exam. What are the odds for passing?

Odds for = 0.40 / 0.60 = 0.667, or "2 to 3." Now flip it: if a second colleague's odds of passing are quoted as "4 to 1," her probability of passing is 4/(4+1) = 0.80.

Check yourself
The odds against a company winning a lawsuit are quoted as "9 to 1." What's the probability the company wins?
Correct — for "odds against = a to b," P(E) = b/(a+b) = 1/(9+1) = 0.10. Winning is the underdog outcome here.
Try it — odds ⇄ probability
Odds for E = 0.25 / 0.75 = 0.33, or "1 to 3"
Odds against E = "3 to 1"
05

Conditional Probability

"What's the chance of A?" versus "what's the chance of A, now that I know B happened?" — two very different questions.

An unconditional (marginal) probability is a plain, standalone likelihood — P(A), no strings attached. A conditional probability, written P(A | B) and read "the probability of A given B," restricts the question: given that we already know B happened, what's now the chance of A?

Definition

P(A | B) = P(AB) / P(B), where P(B) ≠ 0. The joint probability of both A and B happening, divided by how often B happens at all.

Outside example — a deck of cards

P(card is a King) = 4/52 ≈ 0.077 — unconditional. But P(card is a King | card is a face card) = 4/12 ≈ 0.333 — knowing it's a face card (J, Q, K) already narrows the universe from 52 cards down to 12, and 4 of those 12 are Kings. Conditioning shrinks the sample space you're measuring against.

Finance example

Suppose P(stock return exceeds the risk-free rate) = 0.70 unconditionally. Now condition on "the stock's return is positive," which we know happens 80% of the time and includes every outcome that beats the risk-free rate. P(beats risk-free | positive return) = 0.70/0.80 = 0.875. Learning the return was positive at all makes it more likely — not less — that it also beat the risk-free rate.

One more nuance worth internalizing early: conditioning doesn't always change your answer. If knowing B tells you nothing new about A, then P(A|B) simply equals P(A) — that special case is called independence, covered in Module 08.

Check yourself
You roll one fair die. What is P(roll is a 6 | roll is even)?
Correct. Given "even," the sample space shrinks to {2,4,6} — three equally likely outcomes — and exactly one of them is a 6, so P = 1/3.
Formula
P(A | B) = P(AB) / P(B)
requires P(B) ≠ 0

Read direction matters: P(A | B) is not generally equal to P(B | A). "Probability it rained, given the ground is wet" is very different from "probability the ground is wet, given it rained." Keep track of which event is doing the conditioning.

06

Joint Probability & the Multiplication Rule

"What's the chance of A and B, both happening?"

A joint probability, written P(AB), is the probability that both A and B occur. Rearranging Module 05's conditional-probability definition gives you the multiplication rule — the standard tool for building a joint probability out of pieces you already know.

Multiplication rule for probability

P(AB) = P(A | B) × P(B) — equivalently, P(AB) = P(B | A) × P(A)

Outside example — two coin flips

P(first flip is heads) = 0.5. Because coin flips don't influence each other, P(second flip is heads | first was heads) is still 0.5. So P(both heads) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25 — matching what you'd get by just listing HH, HT, TH, TT and counting.

Worked example — mutual fund performance

Among 200 funds ranked as "winners" or "losers" each year, 66% of Year-1 winners repeat as Year-2 winners, and 50% of funds are losers in any given year. What's the joint probability a fund is a loser in both years?

Let A = "Year-2 loser," B = "Year-1 loser." Given P(A | B) = 0.66 and P(B) = 0.50: P(AB) = 0.66 × 0.50 = 0.33. About a third of all funds are losers in both years — noticeably above the 0.25 you'd expect if performance were pure chance, which hints that performance persists somewhat from year to year.

Check yourself
P(it rains today) = 0.30. P(you carry an umbrella | it rains) = 0.90. What's P(it rains AND you carry an umbrella)?
Correct — P(AB) = P(A|B) × P(B) = 0.90 × 0.30 = 0.27.
Formula
P(AB) = P(A|B) · P(B)
= P(B|A) · P(A)
07

The Addition Rule

"What's the chance of A or B?" — where "or" means at least one of them happens.

If you naively add P(A) + P(B), you double-count any outcomes the two events share. The addition rule corrects for that overlap by subtracting the joint probability once.

Addition rule for probability

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(AB)

If A and B are mutually exclusive, P(AB) = 0, and the rule collapses to the simple version you may already know intuitively: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).

Outside example — a standard deck

P(card is a King) = 4/52. P(card is a Heart) = 13/52. These overlap at exactly one card, the King of Hearts: P(King and Heart) = 1/52. So P(King or Heart) = 4/52 + 13/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 ≈ 0.308. Forgetting to subtract the overlap would have wrongly counted the King of Hearts twice.

Finance example

Over 150 trading days, the stock index rose on 80 days (P(A) = 0.533) and interest rates fell on 30 days (P(B) = 0.20); both happened together on 15 days (P(AB) = 0.10). P(index rose OR rates fell) = 0.533 + 0.20 − 0.10 = 0.633 — roughly 63% of days had at least one of the two events occur.

Check yourself
You roll one die. What's P(roll is even OR roll is greater than 4)?
Correct. Even = {2,4,6}, P=3/6. Greater than 4 = {5,6}, P=2/6. Overlap = {6}, P=1/6. So P(even OR >4) = 3/6 + 2/6 − 1/6 = 4/6.
Formula
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(AB)
if mutually exclusive: P(AB)=0
08

Independent vs. Dependent Events

Does knowing about B change what you believe about A? If not, they're independent.

Definition

Events A and B are independent if and only if P(A | B) = P(A), equivalently P(B | A) = P(B). Knowing one occurred tells you nothing about the other. Otherwise, they're dependent.

Independence has a shortcut version of the multiplication rule: since P(A | B) collapses to just P(A), you can skip conditioning entirely.

Multiplication rule for independent events
P(AB) = P(A) × P(B)
Outside example

Two separate dice rolls are independent — the first roll has zero influence on the second. P(both dice show 6) = (1/6)(1/6) = 1/36. Contrast that with drawing two cards from a deck without replacement: the first draw changes what's left, so those two draws are dependent — P(2nd is an Ace | 1st was an Ace) = 3/51, not the plain 4/52.

Finance example — a stock screen

You apply five independent screening criteria to 905 stocks, with pass rates of 61.4%, 54.0%, 66.3%, 54.1%, and 34.6%. Under independence, the chance a stock clears all five is the product of the individual probabilities:

0.614 × 0.540 × 0.663 × 0.541 × 0.346 ≈ 0.041 — only about 4%, or roughly 37 stocks out of 905. Even when no single criterion is very strict, requiring several independent conditions at once shrinks the surviving pool fast.

Don't confuse "independent" with "mutually exclusive." They're almost opposites. Mutually exclusive events are maximally dependent — if you know one happened, you know for certain the other didn't (P(A|B)=0, not P(A)). True independence means B happening changes nothing about A's likelihood at all.

Check yourself
P(A) = 0.30, P(B) = 0.40. If A and B are independent, what is P(AB)?
Correct — under independence, P(AB) = P(A) × P(B) = 0.30 × 0.40 = 0.12.

The Total Probability Rule

Break a hard question into scenarios, solve each easy piece, then reassemble the answer as a weighted average.

Often you can't estimate P(A) directly, but you can estimate it separately under a few different scenarios. If those scenarios are mutually exclusive and exhaustive — they cover every possibility with no overlap — the total probability rule lets you stitch the pieces back into one overall answer.

Two-scenario version

P(A) = P(A | S)·P(S) + P(A | SC)·P(SC)

General, n-scenario version

P(A) = P(A|S₁)P(S₁) + P(A|S₂)P(S₂) + … + P(A|Sₙ)P(Sₙ), where S₁…Sₙ are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.

In plain terms: the overall probability of A is a weighted average of its probability under each scenario, weighted by how likely each scenario is.

Outside example — two factories

A toy company gets 60% of its units from Factory 1 (2% defect rate) and 40% from Factory 2 (5% defect rate). What's the overall probability a random toy is defective? P(defect) = 0.02×0.60 + 0.05×0.40 = 0.012 + 0.020 = 0.032, a 3.2% overall defect rate — a blend of the two factories' rates, weighted by how much each one supplies.

Finance example — forecasting EPS

You know: P(EPS rises next quarter) = 0.55 overall; P(prior quarter's EPS rose) = 0.55; and P(EPS rises | prior quarter fell) = 0.40. What must P(EPS rises | prior quarter rose) be, for these numbers to stay internally consistent?

0.55 = P(A|S)(0.55) + 0.40(0.45) → P(A|S) = [0.55 − 0.18] / 0.55 ≈ 0.673. Since this conditional probability (67.3%) is well above the unconditional 55%, the data implies some persistence — an EPS increase tends to be followed by another increase.

Check yourself
40% of a bond portfolio is investment-grade (2% default rate), 60% is high-yield (8% default rate). What is the overall default rate?
Correct — 0.02×0.40 + 0.08×0.60 = 0.008 + 0.048 = 0.056, or 5.6%.

Principles of Counting — Multiplication Rule & Factorial

Before you can find an a priori probability, you often need to count how many possibilities exist in the first place.

When outcomes are equally likely, a priori probability = (favorable outcomes) / (total outcomes). Counting those outcomes by hand gets unmanageable fast, so a small toolkit of counting shortcuts does the heavy lifting.

Multiplication rule for counting

If task 1 can be done n₁ ways, task 2 (given task 1) can be done n₂ ways, … task k can be done nk ways, then all k tasks together can be done n₁ × n₂ × … × nk ways.

Outside example — building an outfit

3 shirts, 4 pairs of pants, 2 pairs of shoes → 3 × 4 × 2 = 24 different complete outfits. Each choice is independent of the others, so you just multiply straight through.

A special, very common case: assigning every member of a group to its own unique slot, one at a time. The first item has n choices, the next has (n−1) remaining, and so on down to 1. That chain of shrinking choices is n factorial.

Factorial

n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × … × 1, and by convention 0! = 1.

Finance example

You need to assign 3 analysts, one each, to cover 3 different industries. The first analyst can be assigned 3 ways, the second (only 2 industries left) 2 ways, the last analyst just 1 way: 3 × 2 × 1 = 3! = 6 possible assignments.

Check yourself
A restaurant menu has 4 starters, 6 mains, and 3 desserts. How many different 3-course meals can you build (one item from each course)?
Correct — multiplication rule for counting: 4 × 6 × 3 = 72.

Labeling Problems & the Combination Formula

When order doesn't matter — sorting items into groups, or choosing a subset from a larger pool.

Sometimes you're not lining objects up one after another — you're sorting them into labeled buckets, where the order within a bucket doesn't matter. That's a labeling problem, and its general formula is the multinomial formula.

Multinomial formula (general labeling)

Ways to label n objects into k groups of sizes n₁, n₂, …, nk (where n₁+n₂+…+nk=n):

n! / (n₁! × n₂! × … × nk!)

Outside example — sorting a class

A teacher wants to split 10 students into a 4-person group, a 3-person group, and a 3-person group for a project. Number of ways = 10! / (4!×3!×3!) = 3,628,800 / (24×6×6) = 4,200 distinct ways to form the three teams.

The special case where there are only two labels — "in the group" and "not in the group" — is so common it gets its own name: the combination formula.

Combination formula ("n choose r")

ₙCᵣ = n! / [(n−r)! × r!] — the number of ways to choose r objects from n, when order does not matter.

Finance example — option pricing paths

In a binomial model with 5 up/down price moves, how many distinct sequences contain exactly 3 up-moves? Order of the moves doesn't matter for the final price, only the count — so use ₅C₃ = 5!/(2!×3!) = 120/12 = 10 equally likely sequences.

Check yourself
A committee of 3 people is chosen from a pool of 8 candidates, with no distinct roles. How many different committees are possible?
Correct — order doesn't matter for committee membership, so use ₈C₃ = 8!/(5!×3!) = 56.

Permutations — When Order Matters

Same idea as a combination, but now who's first, second, and third actually matters.

A permutation is an ordered subset of objects. Choosing "1st place, 2nd place, 3rd place" from a group is a permutation problem — swap two winners' positions and you get a genuinely different result, unlike a combination.

Permutation formula

ₙPᵣ = n! / (n−r)! — the number of ways to choose and arrange r objects from n, when order does matter.

Outside example — a race podium

8 runners compete; only gold, silver, and bronze matter (in order). ₈P₃ = 8!/5! = 8×7×6 = 336 possible podium arrangements. Compare that to ₈C₃ = 56 if you only cared which three runners medaled, regardless of order — permutations are always larger, since each combination can be arranged r! different ways.

Finance example — annual report awards

A panel of judges picks 1st-, 2nd-, and 3rd-place winners from 5 finalist companies' annual reports. Because rank matters, this is a permutation: ₅P₃ = 5!/2! = 5×4×3 = 60 possible ways to hand out the three placements — six times more than the ₅C₃ = 10 ways you'd get if the judges were just picking 3 winners with no ranking.

The relationship between them
ₙPᵣ = ₙCᵣ × r!

Every combination of r items can be reordered r! ways, which is exactly the gap between "how many groups" and "how many ordered groups."

Check yourself
4 finalists remain for Gold, Silver, and Bronze medals. How many different medal outcomes are possible?
Correct — ₄P₃ = 4!/1! = 24. (Or reason directly: 4 choices for gold × 3 remaining for silver × 2 remaining for bronze = 24.)
