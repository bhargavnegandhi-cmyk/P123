You never see the whole population. Here's how to estimate it honestly.

An index tracks a sample of stocks, not every stock on earth. A survey calls a hundred clients, not all of them. Every number an analyst reports from data is really a guess about something larger and unseen — and this module is about making that guess as honest and as precisely bounded as possible, and about the sneaky ways a sample can lie to you.

01

Point Estimates of a Population Parameter

Two branches of statistical inference ask two different questions.

Two branches of statistical inference

Hypothesis testing asks: "Does the evidence support a specific claimed value for this parameter?" (covered in the next module)

Estimation asks: "What is this parameter's value?" — with no starting claim to test, just a best guess built from sample data.

Estimation itself comes in two flavors. A point estimate is a single best-guess number — the sample mean X̄ used as an estimate of the unknown population mean μ. A confidence interval instead gives a range of plausible values, which is usually far more informative, since it also communicates how uncertain the estimate is.

Estimator vs. estimate

An estimator is the formula itself (e.g., "add up the observations and divide by n") — since it depends on which random sample you happen to draw, the estimator is a random variable with its own sampling distribution. An estimate is the one fixed number you get from plugging in a specific sample — it has no sampling distribution of its own, because it's already just a number.

Outside example — polling

"Percent of registered voters who support Candidate X" is the population parameter. "Ask a random 1,000 voters and compute the percentage" is the estimator. Whatever number that particular poll returns — say, 52% — is the estimate. Poll again with a different random 1,000 voters, and you'd likely get a slightly different estimate; that variability across repeated samples is exactly the sampling distribution.

Check yourself
An analyst calculates the average P/E ratio of a random sample of 50 stocks and gets 18.3. Is 18.3 an estimator or an estimate?
Correct — 18.3 is one fixed number computed from one particular sample. The formula "average the sample" is the estimator; 18.3 is what that formula produced this time — the estimate.

Why indexes matter here: the S&P 500 doesn't contain every US stock, and the Nikkei 225 doesn't contain every Japanese stock — both are samples used to estimate something about their whole markets. Every statistic built from either index is, technically, an estimate of a broader population parameter.

02

Properties of a Good Estimator

Not all formulas for estimating the same parameter are equally trustworthy. Three properties separate the good ones.

Unbiasedness

An estimator is unbiased if the mean of its sampling distribution equals the true parameter it's estimating. The sample mean X̄ is an unbiased estimator of μ. The sample variance s² — using the n−1 divisor — is an unbiased estimator of σ²; dividing by n instead would systematically understate the true variance.

Efficiency

Among all unbiased estimators of a parameter, the efficient one has the smallest sampling-distribution variance — its estimates cluster more tightly around the true value across repeated samples. X̄ is not just unbiased but efficient for μ.

Consistency

A consistent estimator's sampling distribution collapses onto the true parameter value as the sample size grows toward infinity — its standard error shrinks toward zero. Even a biased estimator can be consistent, if that bias vanishes as n grows.

A Big Data nuance: with very large datasets, consistency often matters more than efficiency — since a biased-but-consistent estimator's error shrinks toward zero anyway as more data arrives, while an efficient-but-inconsistent estimator offers no such guarantee.

Check yourself
An estimator's sampling distribution is centered away from the true population parameter at every sample size, but that gap shrinks toward zero as the sample size grows toward infinity. This estimator is:
Correct — bias means the sampling distribution's mean doesn't equal the true parameter at any finite sample size. But if that gap disappears as n → ∞, the estimator is still consistent.
PropertyHolds for
UnbiasedAny sample size
EfficientAny sample size
ConsistentLarge-sample (asymptotic) behavior
03

Confidence Interval Structure & Interpretation

Every confidence interval in this module has exactly the same skeleton.

The universal structure
Point estimate  ±  Reliability factor  ×  Standard error

The reliability factor depends on the assumed distribution of the point estimate and the desired degree of confidence, 1−α. The standard error is the standard deviation of the point estimate's own sampling distribution. Together, "reliability factor × standard error" is called the interval's precision — a wider precision means a less precise (less informative) estimate.

Two interpretations of a 95% confidence interval

Probabilistic: if you repeated the sampling process many times and built a 95% CI from each sample, about 95% of those intervals would contain the true population mean.

Practical: since you don't actually repeat the process, you instead say you're "95% confident" that this one interval contains the true mean — justified because you know 95% of all intervals built this way would.

Common misreading: a 95% CI does not mean "there's a 95% chance the true mean falls in this specific range." The true mean is a fixed (if unknown) number — it's either in the interval or it isn't. The 95% describes the reliability of the method, not the probability attached to this one already-computed interval.

Check yourself
Holding everything else fixed, what happens to a confidence interval's width as you increase the degree of confidence from 90% to 99%?
Correct — a higher degree of confidence requires a larger reliability factor, which widens the interval. You trade precision for confidence.

This module only covers two-sided confidence intervals — the kind with both a lower and an upper bound, symmetric around the point estimate.

04

Confidence Intervals — Known Population Variance

The cleanest, simplest case — and the one all the others are built from.

Sampling from a normal distribution with a known population variance σ², the reliability factor comes straight from the standard normal distribution. The notation zα means "the point beyond which α of the probability sits in the right tail."

Confidence interval — known variance
X̄ ± zα/2 · (σ / √n)
Confidence levelReliability factor
90%z₀.₀₅ = 1.65
95%z₀.₀₂₅ = 1.96
99%z₀.₀₀₅ = 2.58
Worked example

A sample of n=100 from a normal population with known σ²=400 (σ=20) has X̄=25. Standard error = 20/√100 = 2.

95% CI = 25 ± 1.96(2) = 25 ± 3.92 = [21.08, 28.92].

Check yourself
A sample of n=64 from a normal population with known σ=16 has X̄=50. What is the 90% confidence interval?
Correct — standard error = 16/√64 = 2. CI = 50 ± 1.65(2) = 50 ± 3.30 = [46.70, 53.30].
05

Confidence Intervals — Unknown Population Variance

The realistic case — in practice, you almost never actually know σ.

With σ unknown, you estimate it with the sample standard deviation s instead. Two approaches are then available.

The t-distribution approach (theoretically correct)
X̄ ± tα/2 · (s / √n)   df = n−1

Valid whenever the sample is large, or the sample is small but the underlying population is (approximately) normal. It's the standard, most conservative choice in investment practice — even with a large sample, where the z-alternative below would technically be permitted, the t-distribution gives a wider, more cautious interval.

The z-alternative (large sample only, n ≥ 30 as a rule of thumb)
X̄ ± zα/2 · (s / √n)

Justified by the central limit theorem: with a large enough sample, the sampling distribution of X̄ is approximately normal regardless of the shape of the underlying population — so a z reliability factor is an acceptable approximation.

Worked example — Sharpe ratios, both approaches

n=100 mutual funds, X̄ = 0.45, s = 0.30. Standard error = 0.30/√100 = 0.03. For a 90% CI:

z-alternative: z₀.₀₅ = 1.65 → 0.45 ± 1.65(0.03) = 0.45 ± 0.0495 = [0.4005, 0.4995].

t-distribution (df=99): t₀.₀₅ ≈ 1.66 → 0.45 ± 1.66(0.03) = 0.45 ± 0.0498 = [0.4002, 0.4998]. Nearly identical here, since with 99 degrees of freedom the t-distribution has already converged very close to the normal.

Which reliability factor to use, at a glance.

Check yourself
You have a small sample (n=15) from a population whose variance is unknown and whose shape is unknown (not confirmed normal). What reliability factor should you use?
Correct — with a small sample from a non-normal (or unconfirmed) distribution, neither the CLT (needs a large sample) nor the small-sample t-distribution (needs population normality) applies.

06

Selecting Sample Size

Bigger samples give narrower, more precise confidence intervals — but "bigger is always better" has real limits.

Standard error shrinks with √n
Standard error of the mean = s / √n

Define E = Reliability factor × Standard error — half the width of the confidence interval. Solving for the sample size needed to hit a target precision E:

Required sample size
n = [(reliability factor × s) / E]²
Worked example — narrowing a fund-flow estimate

A money manager surveys clients about planned cash flows. With n=10 (df=9), X̄=5.5%, s=10%: 95% CI = 5.5% ± 2.262(3.162%) = [−1.65%, 12.65%], a width of about 14.3 percentage points.

Bump the sample to n=30 (same mean and s): 95% CI = 5.5% ± 2.045(1.826%) = [1.77%, 9.23%] — the width shrinks to about 52% of the original. Tripling the sample size roughly halved the interval's width, not a third of it, because precision scales with √n, not n.

Why not always maximize sample size?
  • Multiple populations: a larger sample may quietly pool together data that actually comes from more than one underlying population (different means or variances), muddying the estimate.
  • Cost: collecting more data has real expense that may outweigh the value of extra precision.
Check yourself
All else equal, if you increase the sample size from n to 4n, approximately how does the standard error change?
Correct — standard error scales with 1/√n, so quadrupling n halves the standard error: √(1/4) = 1/2.

07

Data Snooping Bias

If you torture the data long enough, it will confess to something — whether or not it's true.

Definition

Data snooping is repeatedly searching the same dataset for statistically significant patterns until something appears to work — then reporting only that "discovery," without mentioning the many other patterns that were tried and failed.

With enough variables tested, something will look statistically significant purely by chance — that's a mathematical certainty, not evidence of a real effect. The standard defense is to split data into three parts:

DatasetPurpose
TrainingBuild the model, fit its parameters
ValidationTune the model while checking fit
TestA genuine out-of-sample check on the final model

A variable born from data snooping typically fails in genuine out-of-sample tests — the pattern doesn't repeat because it was never real, just noise. Even a strategy that does hold up out-of-sample isn't a guaranteed win: the ultimate test is future performance, and if the strategy becomes widely known, other investors may trade it away.

Two warning signs of data mining (McQueen & Thorley)

Too much digging, too little confidence: phrases like "we noticed that…" hint the researcher tried many variables before finding one that worked.

No story, no future: a significant variable with no plausible economic rationale behind it is unlikely to keep working — a story alone isn't sufficient either, but its total absence is a red flag.

Check yourself
A published trading strategy performs well in-sample but is never tested on a separate out-of-sample dataset. This is most suggestive of:
Correct — without an out-of-sample test, there's no way to distinguish a genuine pattern from one that was simply mined out of the training data by chance.

Intergenerational data mining: even researchers who don't personally over-test a dataset can fall victim by building on prior researchers' well-known findings from that same dataset — inheriting the bias secondhand.

08

Sample Selection & Survivorship Bias

When data availability quietly decides who gets counted, the sample stops representing the population.

Sample selection bias

Occurs whenever data availability causes certain securities, funds, or time periods to be systematically excluded from an analysis.

Survivorship bias — the most common special case

A database that only tracks currently-existing companies or funds silently drops everything that failed, delisted, or shut down — inflating the apparent average performance of "the market" or "the strategy," since only the winners are left to measure.

A classic survivorship-bias pattern: among surviving stocks only, low P/B predicts higher returns (downward-sloping line). Add back the failed low-P/B stocks that got dropped from the database, and the relationship all but disappears (flat line).

Where else this shows up

Hedge fund databases are especially exposed: funds choose whether to report, so poor performers quietly stop reporting (self-selection), and databases often drop funds that close entirely (survivorship). A related issue, backfill bias, arises when a new fund's already-good historical track record gets retroactively added to an index right when it joins — inflating the index's apparent past performance.

Check yourself
A study of "successful" mutual funds over the last 20 years uses a database that only includes funds still operating today. This is most likely to suffer from:
Correct — funds that closed or merged away are excluded simply because they no longer exist, inflating the average performance of the funds that remain.

Even "good" data isn't immune: stock exchanges with stricter listing requirements (like the NYSE) implicitly select for higher-quality companies — a subtler, less obvious version of the same underlying problem.

09

Look-Ahead Bias & Time-Period Bias

Two more ways a backtest can quietly cheat — by using information from the future, or by choosing a window that flatters the result.

Look-ahead bias

A test design uses information that would not actually have been available on the test date. Classic example: using a company's fiscal year-end book value to compute a P/B ratio "as of" year-end — even though that book value typically isn't publicly reported until well into the following quarter.

The fix — point-in-time (PIT) data

PIT data is stamped with the date it actually became publicly available (e.g., the filing date), not the date it conceptually describes — so a backtest only ever "sees" information a real investor could have seen on that date.

A subtler version: normalizing data by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation of the validation or test set itself (rather than the training set) silently leaks future information into the normalization — a hidden form of look-ahead bias.

Time-period bias

Results are specific to the particular time period tested, rather than reflecting a persistent, general relationship. A short window risks period-specific noise; a long window risks straddling a structural break — a regime change in volatility or interest rates — that blends two genuinely different return distributions into one misleading average.

Check yourself
A backtest ranks stocks each quarter using year-end book value figures that were, in reality, not publicly released until 10 weeks after year-end — but the backtest uses them as of the year-end date. This is:
Correct — the backtest is using information (the book value) before it was actually available to real investors, which is the definition of look-ahead bias.
