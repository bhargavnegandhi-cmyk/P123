A disciplined way to say "the data disagrees with you."

Every quant module in this toolkit has been building toward this moment. Hypothesis testing is the formal machinery for asking: given what I've observed in a sample, should I believe a specific claim about the whole population — or does the evidence make that claim hard to sustain? This module builds the full six-step framework, then applies it to the five tests analysts reach for constantly.

01

Why Hypothesis Testing?

A picture of the data raises questions. Hypothesis testing is how you answer them rigorously.

Plot 33 years of returns for two investments and you'll see two wandering lines. But eyeballing a chart can't answer precise questions: Is Investment One's average return really different from 5%? Is Investment Two more volatile than Investment One? Hypothesis testing is the set of tools built specifically for making judgments like these about a population, using only a sample.

The core intuition — implications from a sampling distribution

Imagine 1,000 historical returns with a true mean of 6% and standard deviation of 2%. Draw a random sample of 30 and you might get a sample mean of 6.13% — reassuringly close. Draw another sample of 30 and you might get 4.80% — a lot further away. Repeat this 100 times and you'll see sample means scattered anywhere from about 5.06% to 7.03%, purely from sampling variation, even though the true population mean never moved from 6%.

100 sample means, each computed from a different random sample of 30 observations drawn from the same population (true mean 6%). The spread is real — and it's exactly what hypothesis testing has to account for.

That spread is the whole problem hypothesis testing solves: a sample mean of 4.80% doesn't prove the population mean isn't 6% — it might just be an unlucky draw. Hypothesis testing gives you an objective, probability-based rule for deciding when a sample result is surprising enough to doubt the claimed population value, and when it isn't.

Check yourself
Even when the true population mean is exactly 6%, why might a random sample of 30 observations produce a sample mean of 4.80%?
Correct — every sample mean is a random variable with its own sampling distribution; a result this far from the truth is less likely, but entirely possible by chance alone.

Estimation vs. hypothesis testing: the previous module (Sampling & Estimation) asked "what is this parameter's value?" This module instead asks "does the evidence support a specific claimed value?" — a sharper, more targeted question.

02

The Process of Hypothesis Testing & Stating the Hypotheses

Six steps, always in the same order — and the very first one shapes everything after it.

The six-step process

1. State the hypotheses  →  2. Identify the test statistic  →  3. Specify the significance level  →  4. State the decision rule  →  5. Collect data & calculate the test statistic  →  6. Make a decision.

Every test states two competing hypotheses. The null hypothesis (H₀) is the claim assumed true unless the sample gives convincing evidence otherwise — it's what you're actually trying to reject. The alternative hypothesis (Hₐ) is what you conclude if you do reject the null.

Two-sided vs. one-sided tests

Two-sided: H₀: μ = μ₀ vs. Hₐ: μ ≠ μ₀ — asks only "is it different," in either direction.

One-sided (right): H₀: μ ≤ μ₀ vs. Hₐ: μ > μ₀ — asks specifically "is it greater."

One-sided (left): H₀: μ ≥ μ₀ vs. Hₐ: μ < μ₀ — asks specifically "is it less."

Notice the null always includes the equality sign (≤, ≥, or =) — the null and alternative must be mutually exclusive and, together, cover every possibility. And however the hypotheses are framed, the actual calculation always tests at the point of equality, μ = μ₀ — if the evidence is strong enough to reject "equals," it's automatically strong enough to reject "less than or equal" too.

Worked example — choosing the right formulation

An analyst suspects excess stock returns have recently fallen below 5% and wants to test that specifically. Which formulation fits?

H₀: μ ≥ 5 versus Hₐ: μ < 5 — a one-sided (left) test. The alternative should state what the analyst actually suspects and wants evidence for; the null is what she's trying to reject. A common shortcut: write the alternative hypothesis first, since it usually reflects the belief being tested, then set the null as its complement.

Check yourself
A researcher believes a risk premium is positive and wants strong evidence specifically for "greater than zero," not just "different from zero." Which hypotheses fit best?
Correct — a one-sided (right) test directly targets "greater than zero," matching the researcher's specific belief, with the null stated as its logical complement.

You never "accept" a null hypothesis. The only two outcomes are "reject the null" or "fail to reject the null" — language that keeps you honest about the asymmetry: absence of evidence against H₀ isn't the same as proof that H₀ is true.

Significance Level, Type I & Type II Errors, and Power

The vocabulary

Significance level (α): the probability of a Type I error — rejecting a true null. Its complement, 1−α, is the confidence level.

Type II error probability (β): failing to reject a false null.

Power of the test (1−β): the probability of correctly rejecting a false null — the complement of a Type II error.

The unavoidable trade-off: shrinking α (say, from 5% to 1%) makes Type I errors rarer, but mechanically makes Type II errors more likely, since you'll reject the null less often — including when it's genuinely false. The only way to reduce both error rates simultaneously is to increase the sample size.

Because β is hard to pin down (there are countless different "false" values the true parameter might actually be), practice typically specifies only α up front — commonly 10%, 5%, or 1%, chosen before calculating the test statistic, precisely so the result itself can't quietly influence the choice.

Check yourself
All else equal, if an analyst lowers the significance level from 5% to 1%, what happens to the probability of a Type II error?
Correct — a stricter significance level means you reject the null less often, which raises the chance of failing to reject a false null — a Type II error.

05

Decision Rules, Critical Values & Confidence Intervals

Where exactly is "too far"? Critical values draw the line before you ever see the data.

A decision rule is set up in advance: reject H₀ if the test statistic falls beyond the critical value(s). For a two-sided test at significance α, the critical values split α equally between both tails, denoted ±zα/2 (or ±tα/2). For a one-sided test, there's a single critical value, zα, on the relevant side.

Same question, two equivalent methods

Testing H₀: μ = μ₀ at 95% confidence, you can either (1) compare the calculated test statistic to ±1.96, or (2) build a 95% confidence interval around X̄ and check whether μ₀ falls inside it. Both methods always agree: if μ₀ sits outside the confidence interval, the test statistic sits outside ±1.96, and the null is rejected either way.

Worked example

Testing H₀: μ = 3 vs. Hₐ: μ ≠ 3, a sample produces a 95% CI of [2.75, 4.25]. Since the hypothesized value 3 falls comfortably inside that interval, the decision is to fail to reject the null — there's not enough evidence the mean differs from 3.

Check yourself
Testing H₀: μ = 10 at the 95% confidence level, the sample's 95% confidence interval is [10.4, 12.1]. What's the decision?
Correct — the hypothesized value 10 falls outside the confidence interval [10.4, 12.1], so the null is rejected.

Data quality matters too: step 5 of the process — collecting data and calculating the test statistic — depends on a sample free of biases like sample selection or time-period bias (covered in the Sampling & Estimation module), and on clean, error-checked data. A perfect statistical framework can't rescue a flawed sample.

06

Making a Decision — Statistical vs. Economic Significance

Rejecting the null is a statistical verdict. Whether that verdict is worth acting on is a separate question entirely.

The statistical decision is mechanical: compare the test statistic to the critical value and reject or fail to reject. The economic decision layers judgment on top — does the result matter enough, after transaction costs, taxes, and risk, to actually change what you do?

Why the two can diverge: a test statistic's value depends heavily on the standard error, which shrinks as sample size grows. With a large enough sample, even a tiny, economically trivial difference from the hypothesized value can become statistically significant. Statistical significance is not the same thing as a result worth trading on.

Worked example

Testing H₀: μ ≤ 0% vs. Hₐ: μ > 0% for a trading strategy's risk-adjusted return: sample mean 0.7%, calculated t-statistic 2.428, critical t-value 2.345. Transaction costs run 0.3%.

Statistically: 2.428 > 2.345, so reject the null — the mean return is greater than zero. Economically: 0.7% − 0.3% = 0.4% net of costs, still positive — so the result is both statistically and economically significant. Had transaction costs instead been 0.8%, the same statistical conclusion would hold, but the strategy would no longer be worth pursuing.

Check yourself
A massive sample (n=50,000) finds a trading strategy's mean return is statistically significantly different from zero, at 0.02% per trade. Transaction costs run 0.15% per trade. This result is most likely:
Correct — a huge sample can make even a razor-thin 0.02% edge statistically significant, but it doesn't come close to covering a 0.15% transaction cost, so the strategy isn't economically worth pursuing.

Even a result that clears both bars deserves one more question: is there a plausible economic reason this pattern should persist going forward? A hypothesis test can't answer that — it can only tell you what the past sample shows.

07

The Role of p-Values

One number that replaces "reject or not" with "exactly how much evidence is there."

Definition

The p-value is the probability of the area in the distribution beyond the calculated test statistic — for a two-sided test, beyond ± the statistic; for a one-sided test, beyond it on the relevant side only. Equivalently, it's the smallest significance level at which the null could still be rejected.

The rule is simple: if the p-value is less than α, reject the null. A smaller p-value means stronger evidence against H₀.

p-value = 1.98%
Worked example

A calculated z-statistic of 2.33 on a two-sided test gives a p-value of about 1.98% — the combined area beyond +2.33 and beyond −2.33. Since 1.98% < 5%, the null is rejected at the 5% significance level. Note that this is a stricter statement than just "outside ±1.96" — the p-value tells you exactly how much evidence you have, not just whether you cleared the bar.

Check yourself
A test produces a p-value of 3%. At which significance levels would you reject the null?
Correct — reject whenever α exceeds the p-value. 3% is less than both 5% and 10%, but greater than 1%, so the null is rejected at 5% and 10% but not at 1%.

A useful mental model: if the true null hypothesis were repeatedly tested on many different random samples, the resulting p-values would be roughly uniformly spread between 0% and 100%. But if the null is actually false, p-values pile up sharply near 0% instead — the further the true value is from the hypothesized one, the more extreme that pile-up becomes, which is exactly what gives a test its power.

08

Multiple Tests & Significance Interpretation

Run enough tests, and pure chance will hand you "significant" results — even when nothing real is going on.

At a 5% significance level, a true null hypothesis gets rejected by chance about 5% of the time — that's what α means. Run 100 independent tests on data where nothing is actually going on, and you'd expect roughly 5 "significant" results purely by luck. This is the multiple testing problem, and the resulting fraction of false "discoveries" is the false discovery rate (FDR).

The Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) correction

Rank all p-values from smallest to largest. Starting from the smallest (rank i=1), check whether p₍ᵢ₎ ≤ α × (i / total number of tests). Find the largest rank k where this still holds — every test ranked at or below k is considered genuinely significant; everything ranked above k is not, regardless of its raw p-value.

Worked example — 20 samples, six lowest p-values
Rank (i)p-valueα × i/20Significant?
10.00140.0025Yes
20.02470.0050No
30.03580.0075No
40.03790.0100No

Naively using a flat 5% cutoff on each test individually would call all four of these "significant." Applying the BH correction, only rank 1 actually clears its adjusted bar — just one test survives as genuinely significant, not four.

Four practical takeaways
  • A non-significant result isn't "wrong" — the null may genuinely be true.
  • Low power or a small sample raises the risk that any "significant" hit is a false positive.
  • Critical values assume the test is run once — repeatedly re-testing the same data risks data snooping.
  • With very large samples, nearly everything looks significant — checking whether a result replicates on a fresh, independent sample is the real test of robustness.
Check yourself
A researcher runs 40 independent hypothesis tests at a 5% significance level on data where every null hypothesis is actually true. About how many "significant" results should she expect purely by chance?
Correct — 40 × 0.05 = 2 expected false positives, purely from running many tests at a 5% significance level.

Multiple testing and data snooping (from the Sampling & Estimation module) are close cousins — both stem from the same root cause: searching hard enough through data will eventually turn up something that looks significant by pure chance.

09

Tests Concerning a Single Mean

The most common hypothesis test in investment practice.

Test statistic (unknown variance — the usual case)
t = (X̄ − μ₀) / (s / √n)     df = n−1

Since the population variance is almost never actually known, the t-distributed statistic is the theoretically correct default — and it's robust to moderate departures from normality, breaking down mainly with outliers or strong skewness. In the rare case σ is genuinely known, or as a large-sample approximation, a z-statistic can be used instead — with a large sample, the two give nearly identical answers anyway.

Worked example — Investment One's returns

Testing H₀: μ = 6 vs. Hₐ: μ ≠ 6 with n=33, X̄=5.2990%, s=1.4284%, at 5% significance (df=32, critical values ±2.037):

t = (5.2990 − 6) / (1.4284/√33) = −0.7010 / 0.2487 = −2.819. Since −2.819 is beyond −2.037, reject the null — the mean return differs from 6%.

Check yourself
Testing H₀: μ = 1.1% vs. Hₐ: μ ≠ 1.1% with n=24, X̄=1.5%, s=3.6%, the calculated t-statistic is 0.544, against critical values of ±2.069. What's the decision?
Correct — 0.544 falls well within ±2.069, so there's insufficient evidence the mean differs from 1.1%.

Test of Differences between Means — Independent Samples

Are two unrelated groups' averages actually different, or could the gap just be noise?

When two samples are independent (unrelated to each other) and drawn from approximately normal populations with equal variances, combine both samples into a pooled variance estimate before testing.

Pooled variance & test statistic
s²ₚ = [(n₁−1)s₁² + (n₂−1)s₂²] / (n₁+n₂−2)

t = [(X̄₁−X̄₂) − (μ₁−μ₂)] / √(s²ₚ/n₁ + s²ₚ/n₂)    df = n₁+n₂−2
Worked example — two time periods of the same index

Period 1: n=445, X̄=0.01775%, s=0.31580%. Period 2: n=859, X̄=0.01134%, s=0.38760%. Testing H₀: μ₁=μ₂ at 5% (df=1,302, critical ±1.962):

s²ₚ = 0.1330. t = (0.01775−0.01134) / √(0.1330/445 + 0.1330/859) = 0.0064/0.0213 = 0.301. Well within ±1.962 — fail to reject; no evidence the two periods' mean returns differ.

Check yourself
Testing the equality of two independent sample means with n₁=25 and n₂=25, how many degrees of freedom does the t-distributed test statistic have?
Correct — df = n₁+n₂−2 = 25+25−2 = 48.
Try it — independent samples t-test
s²ₚ=0.1330 · t=0.301
FAIL TO REJECT the null

Test of Differences between Means — Dependent (Paired) Samples

When the two samples share something in common, testing their differences directly is more powerful.

Paired observations are linked — the same companies before and after a policy change, or two strategies' returns in the same months. Rather than treating them as two independent samples, collapse each pair into its difference dᵢ = X_Aᵢ − X_Bᵢ, and test whether the mean difference is zero (or some other hypothesized value).

Test statistic
t = (d̄ − μ_d0) / s_d̄     where s_d̄ = s_d/√n,   df = n−1

This test is more powerful than the independent-samples test whenever the pairing is genuine — by differencing out whatever the pairs share (the same time period, the same underlying risk factors), it strips away noise that would otherwise obscure the real difference.

Worked example — two precious-metals portfolios

12 quarterly return pairs for Portfolio A and Portfolio B give a mean difference d̄=4.401% and standard deviation of differences s_d=5.718%. Testing H₀: μd=0 at 10% (df=11, critical ±1.796):

s_d̄ = 5.718/√12 = 1.651. t = 4.401/1.651 = 2.662. Beyond 1.796 — reject the null; the two portfolios' mean returns genuinely differ.

Check yourself
Two investment strategies' monthly returns, measured over the same 40 months, are best compared using:
Correct — both strategies' returns in the same months share underlying market risk factors, making them dependent, not independent — the paired test is the right, more powerful tool.
Try it — paired-samples t-test
s_d̄=1.651 · t=2.662
REJECT the null
12

Tests Concerning Variances — Chi-Square & F-Tests

Testing risk itself, not just returns — one variance against a target, or two variances against each other.

Single variance — chi-square test
χ² = (n−1)s² / σ₀²     df = n−1

Because the chi-square distribution is asymmetric and bounded at zero, a two-sided test needs two different-looking critical values (not a simple ±) — the lower one from the left tail, the upper one from the right.

Worked example — a fund's return volatility

Sendar Equity Fund: n=24, s=3.60% (s²=12.96). Testing H₀: σ²=16 vs. Hₐ: σ²≠16 at 5% (df=23, critical values 11.689 and 38.076):

χ² = 23(12.96)/16 = 18.630. Between 11.689 and 38.076 — fail to reject; no evidence the variance differs from 16.

Two variances — F-test
F = s₁² / s₂²     df₁ = n₁−1, df₂ = n₂−1

Convention: put the larger sample variance in the numerator to keep the F-statistic ≥ 1, simplifying which critical value you need.

Worked example — comparing two investments' risk

Investment One: s=1.4284% (n=33). Investment Two: s=2.5914% (n=33). Testing whether the variances differ, at 5% (df 32,32; critical values 0.494 and 2.025):

F = (2.5914)² / (1.4284)² = 3.291. Beyond 2.025 — reject the null; Investment Two is genuinely more volatile.

Check yourself
Why can't a chi-square or F-test's two-sided critical values simply be written as "±" some number, the way a z- or t-test's can?
Correct — since chi-square and F distributions can't go negative and aren't symmetric, the two tails require two genuinely different critical values, not a mirrored ± pair.
Try it — variance tests
χ² = 23(12.96)/16 = 18.630
FAIL TO REJECT the null
F = 3.291
