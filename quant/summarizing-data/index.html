01

Measures of Central Tendency — Mean, Median, Mode

Three different answers to "what's a typical value in this dataset?" — each with its own strengths and blind spots.

Arithmetic mean

Sum every observation, divide by the count. Uses all the information in the sample — and is therefore also the most sensitive to extreme values.

Median

The middle value once sorted. Odd n: the (n+1)/2 position. Even n: the average of the two middle positions. Unaffected by extreme values — a genuine advantage when a distribution isn't symmetric.

Mode

The most frequently occurring value. A dataset can be unimodal, bimodal, trimodal, or have no mode at all. It's the only central-tendency measure that works on nominal (unordered categorical) data.

There's a useful physical intuition behind the arithmetic mean: it behaves like the center of gravity of the data. Picture nine observations — 2, 4, 4, 6, 10, 10, 12, 12, 12 — stacked on a bar at their respective positions, each stack as tall as its frequency. Balance that bar on a single fulcrum, and it only balances when the fulcrum sits exactly at the mean, 72/9 = 8.

That balancing property comes from a fact that's always true, for any dataset: the sum of deviations from the mean always equals zero — the positive and negative distances cancel out exactly. That single property is the foundation everything in the rest of this module builds on: variance, skewness, and kurtosis are all, at their core, different ways of measuring those same deviations.

Why deviations always sum to zero
Σ(Xᵢ − X̄) = ΣXᵢ − nX̄ = ΣXᵢ − ΣXᵢ = 0
When mean, median, and mode disagree — reading skewness

In a perfectly symmetric distribution, mean = median = mode, and skewness — a measure of the lack of symmetry — is exactly zero. Once a distribution leans one way, the three measures pull apart in a predictable order.

Right (positive) skew — a long tail of unusually large values — pulls the mean above the median, which sits above the mode. Left (negative) skew does the reverse. This module doesn't compute skewness itself, but recognizing the pattern from how mean and median compare is often enough to flag it.

Outliers, Trimmed Means & Winsorized Means

The arithmetic mean's biggest weakness — and three legitimate ways to handle it.

Because every observation counts equally toward the arithmetic mean, a single extreme value can drag it a long way from where most of the data actually sits.

The classic illustration

Take the seven numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 1,000. The mean is 1,021/7 ≈ 145.86 — a number nowhere near where six of the seven observations actually sit. The median, unmoved by the outlier, is a far more representative 4.

In practice, an extreme value might be perfectly legitimate (a rare but real event), or it might signal a data error, or an observation that actually belongs to a different population entirely. The first step is always to investigate — inspect the raw data, or use the visualization tools from Part 1 — and clean any genuine errors. After that, there are three defensible options:

Three ways to handle a confirmed, legitimate outlier

Option 1 — do nothing: keep the data as-is. Appropriate when the value is a real, correct observation and you want the mean to reflect the whole distribution honestly.

Option 2 — trim it: compute a trimmed mean by discarding a stated percentage of the highest and lowest values, then averaging what remains. A 5% trimmed mean drops the lowest 2.5% and highest 2.5%, then averages the middle 95% — the same principle judges use when they discard a contestant's highest and lowest scores.

Option 3 — cap it: compute a winsorized mean by replacing (rather than deleting) extreme values with a specified cutoff value, then averaging the restated data. A 95% winsorized mean sets the bottom 2.5% of values to the 2.5th percentile value, and the top 2.5% to the 97.5th percentile value.

Worked example — five years of EAA Index daily returns (n=1,258)
Arithmetic Mean5% Trimmed Mean95% Winsorized Mean
Mean0.035%0.048%0.038%
Observations used1,2581,1941,258

The trimmed mean drops any return below −1.934% or above 1.671% (the extreme 2.5% on each side), leaving 1,194 observations. The winsorized mean instead caps every return outside that same range at exactly −1.934% or 1.671%, keeping all 1,258 observations. Both adjusted means land above the raw arithmetic mean here — the discarded/capped extreme returns were skewing it downward more than the typical day would suggest.

Weighted, Geometric & Harmonic Mean

The arithmetic mean assumes every observation counts equally. In investments, that assumption often doesn't hold.

Weighted mean

Each observation gets its own weight (weights sum to 1). The arithmetic mean is just the special case where every weight equals 1/n.

Worked example — a portfolio return

$70M in equities (return 8%) and $30M in bonds (return 3%): weight 0.70 on stocks, 0.30 on bonds.

Portfolio return = 0.70(8%) + 0.30(3%) = 5.6% + 0.9% = 6.5% — not the naive (8%+3%)/2 = 5.5%, which would wrongly treat both asset classes as equally important.

Geometric mean

The nth root of the product of n observations. Used to average rates of change or compute compound growth. For returns, add 1 to each decimal return first (so negative returns don't break the math), take the nth root of the product, then subtract 1.

Worked example — compounding returns

A stock returns 7.8%, 6.3%, and −1.5% over three years. Geometric mean = [(1.078)(1.063)(0.985)]^(1/3) − 1 = 1.041189 − 1 ≈ 4.12%.

Compare to the arithmetic mean of (7.8 + 6.3 − 1.5)/3 = 4.20%. The geometric mean is always ≤ the arithmetic mean (equal only when there's zero variability) — and it's the geometric mean that correctly answers "what single constant annual rate would have produced the same final wealth?"

Harmonic mean

The reciprocal of the average of the reciprocals. Appropriate for averaging rates or ratios that are repeatedly applied to a fixed quantity — the classic case being cost averaging (investing a fixed dollar amount at different share prices).

Worked example — cost averaging

You invest €1,000 each month for 2 months, at share prices of €10 and €15. Month 1 buys 100 shares, month 2 buys 66.67 shares — 166.67 shares total for €2,000, an average price of €12.00 per share.

That €12.00 is exactly the harmonic mean of 10 and 15: 2/[(1/10)+(1/15)] = €12.00 — lower than the naive arithmetic average of €12.50, because harmonic averaging automatically gives more weight to the cheaper (more heavily-bought) price.

A useful fact tying all three together: for the same dataset, harmonic mean ≤ geometric mean ≤ arithmetic mean, unless every observation is identical (in which case all three are equal).

Correct — the harmonic mean here is about 19.3, versus an arithmetic mean of 25. Since P/E is a ratio, the harmonic mean is the textbook-appropriate choice and it naturally downweights the large outlier.

Quantiles

The median splits data in half. Quantiles are the more general family of dividing lines — quarters, fifths, tenths, hundredths.

The y-th percentile is the value at or below which y% of observations lie. Quartiles (quarters), quintiles (fifths), and deciles (tenths) are just percentiles with convenient names: the first quartile Q1 is the 25th percentile, Q2 (the median) is the 50th, Q3 is the 75th. The interquartile range IQR = Q3 − Q1 measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data.

Locating a percentile in a sorted array
Ly = (n + 1) × y/100

If Ly is a whole number, that's the exact position in the sorted array. If not, linearly interpolate between the two surrounding positions.

Worked example — 5 years of EAA Index daily returns (n = 1,258)

Ranking all 1,258 daily returns from lowest to highest and locating key percentiles gives:

QuantileValue
Minimum−4.108%
Q1 (25th pctile)−0.293%
Median (50th pctile)0.044%
Q3 (75th pctile)0.460%
Maximum5.001%

IQR = 0.460 − (−0.293) = 0.753%. Notice the median (0.044%) is barely above zero even though the mean was slightly higher (0.035% for the raw mean, from Module 02) — a reminder that mean and median can diverge more sharply in more skewed datasets.

A box-and-whisker plot visualizes exactly this five-number summary. The "box" spans Q1 to Q3 (with the median marked inside), and "whiskers" extend out to the fences — often set at 1.5×IQR beyond the box — with anything past the fences flagged as a potential outlier.

Check yourself
In a sorted array of 39 observations, what is L25 (the location of the 25th percentile)?
Correct — Ly = (39+1) × 25/100 = 40 × 0.25 = 10 exactly, so the 25th percentile is simply the 10th value in the sorted array.

Measures of Dispersion

If the mean tells you the reward, dispersion tells you the risk — how spread out the data really is.

Range

Maximum − Minimum. Trivially easy to compute, but uses only two data points and says nothing about the shape in between.

Mean absolute deviation (MAD)

Average of the absolute deviations from the mean. Solves the "deviations always sum to zero" problem (from Module 01) by stripping out the sign rather than squaring.

Sample variance & standard deviation

Variance squares each deviation from the mean (also fixing the sign-cancellation problem) and divides by n − 1, not n — this correction (the "degrees of freedom" adjustment) makes the sample variance an unbiased estimate of the population variance. Standard deviation is just √variance, which brings the units back to match the original data.

Formulas
Range = Max − Min
MAD = Σ|Xᵢ − X̄| / n
s² = Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² / (n − 1)
s = √s²
Worked example — Year 3 country index returns (n = 11)

Returns: 6.1, −1.5, 3.5, 6.2, 3.0, −1.0, −1.2, 3.4, 3.2, −0.9, 1.2 (mean = 2.0%). Sum of squared deviations = 81.64.

Sample variance = 81.64 / 10 = 8.164. Sample standard deviation = √8.164 ≈ 2.857%.

Check yourself
Why does the sample variance formula divide by (n − 1) instead of n?
Correct — once you've used the data to estimate the mean, only n − 1 observations are free to vary independently; dividing by n − 1 (the degrees of freedom) corrects for that.

Downside Deviation & Coefficient of Variation

Standard deviation treats upside and downside surprises the same way. Investors usually don't.

Target (semi)deviation

Only looks at observations that fall below a chosen target — squares those shortfalls, sums them, divides by n − 1, and takes the square root. It answers "how much downside risk relative to my minimum acceptable return?" rather than "how much risk overall?"

Formula
s_target = √[ Σ(Xᵢ − B)² / (n − 1) ]  for all Xᵢ ≤ B
B = the target
Worked example — monthly portfolio returns

Twelve months of returns: 5, 3, −1, −4, 4, 2, 0, 4, 3, 0, 6, 5 (%). Full-sample standard deviation ≈ 2.96%.

Against a 3% target return, only 5 months fall below target, giving a target semideviation of 2.76%. Against a lower, easier-to-clear 2% target, only 4 months fall short, giving a smaller semideviation of 2.20% — raise the bar (the target) and, mechanically, semideviation rises too, since more observations now count as "below."

Coefficient of variation (CV)

CV = s / X̄ — standard deviation expressed per unit of mean. Because it's unitless, it's the right tool for comparing dispersion across datasets with very different scales or units — e.g., two industries' returns on assets with very different average magnitudes.

Worked example — comparing two industries

Industry A's returns on assets have a similar spread to Industry B's in absolute terms, but Industry B's average return is much lower — so the same dollar of dispersion represents proportionally far more risk per unit of return in Industry B. The coefficient of variation captures that instantly, while comparing the two raw standard deviations side by side would miss it entirely.

Check yourself
Fund X has mean return 4% and standard deviation 8%. Fund Y has mean return 12% and standard deviation 18%. Which fund has more risk per unit of return (higher CV)?
Correct — CV(X) = 8/4 = 2.00 vs. CV(Y) = 18/12 = 1.50. Even though Fund Y has the larger raw standard deviation, Fund X carries more risk per unit of expected return.
