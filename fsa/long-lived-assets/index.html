Three companies, one identical machine, three genuinely different profit pictures.

Buy the exact same piece of equipment, use it the exact same way, and depending purely on which depreciation method you pick, your reported profit in Year 1 can differ by hundreds of dollars — with the difference reversing entirely by the asset's final year. This module works through three hypothetical companies depreciating an identical machine three different ways, side by side.

01

Acquisition Costs — What Gets Capitalized

The sticker price is rarely the whole story — everything genuinely needed to get an asset working belongs in its cost.

What belongs in an asset's capitalized cost

The purchase price, plus any costs directly attributable to bringing the asset to its working condition and location — delivery, installation, testing, and professional fees directly tied to acquisition. Once the asset is ready for its intended use, further costs generally stop being capitalized.

Why this genuinely matters

Capitalizing a cost defers its recognition as an expense, spreading it out via depreciation over the asset's useful life. Expensing the identical cost immediately hits net income right away, in full. Same dollar, same transaction — a materially different profit picture depending purely on the classification choice.

Check yourself
A company pays for delivery and professional installation of a new production machine, before it's ready for use. How should these costs be treated?
Correct — costs directly attributable to bringing an asset to its working condition and location, like delivery and installation, are capitalized into its cost.

The two models ahead: the cost model (required under US GAAP, permitted under IFRS) carries assets at historical cost minus accumulated depreciation. The alternative revaluation model (IFRS only) carries assets at fair value — covered in Section 5.

02

Depreciation Methods

Straight-line, accelerated, and units-of-production — three genuinely different ways to spread the same asset's cost across the years it earns its keep.

The three methods

Straight-line: depreciable cost ÷ useful life — identical expense every single year.

Double-declining balance (accelerated): a fixed rate (double the straight-line rate) applied to the beginning-of-year carrying amount — front-loads expense into earlier years.

Units-of-production: depreciable cost × (actual production this period ÷ total estimated productive capacity) — expense tracks actual usage, not the calendar.

Flagship worked example — three companies, one machine

EVEN-LI Co., SOONER Inc., and AZUSED Co. each buy an identical $2,300 machine — 4-year useful life, $100 residual value, 800 total units of estimated capacity, producing 200/300/200/100 units across the four years — but each uses a different depreciation method.

Check yourself
Does the total accumulated depreciation over an asset's entire useful life depend on which depreciation method is chosen?
Correct — every method depreciates the exact same total depreciable cost (cost minus residual value) over the asset's life; the choice only affects which years get more or less expense.

03

How Depreciation Choices Affect Statements & Ratios

Every depreciation assumption ripples through the income statement, the balance sheet, and a whole family of financial ratios at once.

Accelerated depreciation, in the early years, produces:

Lower net income and operating profit margin, but higher total asset turnover, since the asset's carrying value shrinks faster.

Higher operating cash flow, via lower taxes paid on lower taxable income (a genuine cash benefit, not just an accounting one).

Why switching methods mid-life matters to analysts

A company switching from straight-line to an accelerated method partway through an asset's life will show a sudden jump in depreciation expense, dragging down net income and taxes payable in that specific year — while boosting operating cash flow via the resulting tax savings. Recognizing this pattern helps an analyst separate a genuine operating deterioration from a pure accounting policy change.

Check yourself
A company switches from straight-line to an accelerated depreciation method this year. What is the most likely effect on operating cash flow?
Correct — while depreciation itself is non-cash, the higher expense lowers taxable income and actual cash taxes paid, genuinely increasing operating cash flow.

The capitalize-vs-expense choice has the same directional logic: expensing an investment immediately (instead of capitalizing it) lowers current net income but removes future years' depreciation — front-loading the pain, but making subsequent year-over-year profit growth look artificially stronger by comparison.

04

Amortization of Intangible Assets with Finite Lives

Same idea as depreciation, applied to assets you can't physically touch — a customer list, a patent, a license.

The key distinction

Finite-life intangibles (a patent with a legal expiration, a customer list with an estimated useful benefit period) are amortized over their useful life, exactly like depreciation.

Indefinite-life intangibles (goodwill, some trademarks) are never amortized — instead, they're tested for impairment at least annually.

Worked example — a customer list

A direct-mail marketing company acquires a customer list expected to generate benefits for 18 months, with no residual value. Because the benefit period is finite, the list must be capitalized and amortized over management's best estimate of its useful life — 18 months.

A shorter estimated useful life produces higher amortization expense each year, over fewer years — but the total accumulated amortization over the intangible's full life is identical regardless of the useful-life estimate or the method chosen. Only the timing changes, exactly like depreciation.

Check yourself
A company holds goodwill from a past acquisition, deemed to have an indefinite life. How should this goodwill be accounted for?
Correct — indefinite-life intangibles like goodwill are never amortized; instead, they're tested for impairment at least once a year.

Every point made about depreciation method/estimate choices affecting statements and ratios applies identically to amortization — same mechanics, different asset category.

05

The Revaluation Model

An IFRS-only alternative to historical cost — carry the asset at what it's actually worth today, not what was paid for it years ago.

Cost model vs. revaluation model

Cost model: carrying amount = historical cost − accumulated depreciation − any impairment. Required under US GAAP; also permitted under IFRS.

Revaluation model: carrying amount = fair value at the revaluation date − subsequent depreciation − any subsequent impairment. Permitted under IFRS only; US GAAP prohibits it entirely.

An upward revaluation doesn't flow through net income. Instead, it typically goes directly to other comprehensive income (as a revaluation surplus within equity) — connecting straight back to Module 2's comprehensive income and Module 3's AOCI. A downward revaluation, though, is generally recognized as a loss on the income statement, unless it's reversing a prior upward revaluation of that same asset.

Check yourself
Under IFRS, a company revalues a building upward for the first time, reflecting a genuine increase in fair value. Where does this increase typically get recognized?
Correct — a first-time upward revaluation typically bypasses net income, flowing instead through other comprehensive income into equity as a revaluation surplus.

Why revaluation is genuinely rare in practice: most companies, even under IFRS, stick with the cost model — it's simpler, doesn't require ongoing independent valuations, and doesn't introduce the volatility of periodically re-marking assets to market.

06

Investment Property

The same physical building can be a completely different type of asset, purely depending on why the company holds it.

The distinction — purpose, not physical nature

Property, plant & equipment: held for use in producing goods or services, or for administrative purposes.

Investment property: held purely to earn rental income, for capital appreciation, or both — genuinely separate from the company's core operations.

Outside example — two identical office towers

A company occupies one office tower as its own corporate headquarters (PP&E), while owning an identical tower across the street purely to lease out to unrelated tenants for rental income (investment property). Same physical asset type, completely different classification — and IFRS specifically permits investment property to be measured using either the cost model or a fair value model, with fair value changes flowing directly through net income under the fair value model — a genuinely different treatment from ordinary PP&E's revaluation surplus.

Check yourself
A company owns an office building it leases entirely to unrelated third-party tenants for rental income. How should this building be classified?
Correct — a building held purely to earn rental income, rather than for use in the company's own operations, is classified as investment property.

Why the classification matters to analysts: under the fair value model for investment property, unrealized gains and losses hit net income directly — unlike ordinary PP&E revaluations, which bypass net income via OCI. This can make reported earnings genuinely more volatile for real-estate-heavy companies using this model.

07

Impairment & Derecognition

Sometimes an asset's carrying value on the books is simply, provably too high — and the accounting has to admit it, all at once.

Impairment

An asset is impaired when its carrying amount exceeds its recoverable amount — genuinely no longer expected to generate enough future value to justify its current book value. The carrying amount is written down, with the loss recognized on the income statement.

What an impairment loss actually tells an analyst

An impairment loss suggests insufficient depreciation was recognized in prior years — the asset's book value had drifted too high relative to reality, and this write-down is catching it up all at once. Net income in the impairment year is lower, but subsequent years' net profit margins are typically higher, since there's less remaining carrying value left to depreciate going forward. And critically: impairment is a non-cash charge — it does not affect operating cash flow directly.

Derecognition

An asset is derecognized — removed from the balance sheet entirely — when it's disposed of (sold, scrapped) or when no future economic benefits are expected from its use or disposal. Any gain or loss on disposal is the difference between the sale proceeds and the asset's carrying amount at the time, recognized on the income statement.

Check yourself
A company recognizes a significant impairment loss on a piece of equipment this year. What is the most likely effect on operating cash flow?
Correct — like depreciation, an impairment loss is a non-cash expense and doesn't directly affect operating cash flow, even though it reduces net income.

The full picture, tied together: depreciation, amortization, revaluation, impairment, and derecognition are really all the same underlying question, asked at different moments — is this asset's carrying value still an honest reflection of its remaining economic benefit?
