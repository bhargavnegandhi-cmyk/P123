A bond's price is really just Module 1's present value math, wearing a suit.

Everything in this module — issuance, amortization, redemption — traces back to a single idea already familiar from the Interest Rates module in the Quant volume: discount every promised future cash flow back to today. The only genuinely new part is what happens to that number every single year afterward, as the bond's carrying amount drifts steadily toward its face value.

01

Face Value, Coupon Rate & Market Rate

Three numbers define every bond — and it's the gap between two of them that determines everything else in this module.

The vocabulary

Face value (principal/par value): the amount paid to bondholders at maturity.

Coupon rate (nominal/stated rate): the fixed rate written into the bond contract, used to calculate periodic interest payments.

Market rate (effective interest rate): the rate actually demanded by investors for bonds of comparable risk and terms, at the time of issuance.

How a bond is priced
Bond price = PV of coupon payments + PV of face value, both discounted at the market rate

The coupon rate is fixed for the bond's life. The market rate moves constantly with interest rate conditions. Whenever these two rates diverge at issuance, the bond simply won't sell for face value — the market rate is what actually determines price, and the coupon rate just determines the cash payments.

Check yourself
A bond's coupon rate is fixed at 5% for its entire life. What happens to the market rate demanded by investors over time?
Correct — the market rate reflects current conditions and moves continuously, while the coupon rate is locked in at issuance.

Bond prices are often quoted as a percentage of face value — a bond priced at "100" sells at exactly face value; one priced at "95.79" sells at 95.79% of face value (a discount).

02

Bonds Issued at Par, Premium & Discount

The relationship between coupon rate and market rate determines, entirely, whether a bond sells for exactly face value, more, or less.

The three cases

Issued at par: coupon rate = market rate → sales proceeds = face value.

Issued at a premium: coupon rate > market rate → the bond's attractive coupon makes it worth more than face value.

Issued at a discount: coupon rate < market rate → investors demand a lower price to compensate for the below-market coupon.

Worked example — Debond Corp., issued at par

Debond Corp. issues £1,000,000 face value, 5-year bonds, 5% coupon, when the market rate is also 5%. Annual interest payment = £50,000.

Summing the present value of five £50,000 coupons plus the £1,000,000 face value, all at 5%: PV = £216,474 + £783,526 = £1,000,000 — exactly face value, since coupon rate equals market rate.

Check yourself
A bond has a 6% coupon rate, but the market rate for comparable bonds is only 4%. Will this bond sell at par, a premium, or a discount?
Correct — a coupon rate above the market rate means investors will pay more than face value to obtain that attractive coupon.

03

The Effective Interest Method — Amortizing a Discount

The bond's carrying amount doesn't stay fixed after issuance — it drifts, year by year, all the way to face value by maturity.

The effective interest method, each year
Interest expense = Beginning carrying amount × Market rate
Discount amortization = Interest expense − Coupon payment
Ending carrying amount = Beginning + Discount amortization
Flagship worked example — Debond Corp., issued at a discount

Debond issues £1,000,000 face value, 5-year, 5% coupon bonds when the market rate is 6%. Sales proceeds = £957,876 (a £42,124 discount).

Under the effective interest method, why does interest expense on a discount bond increase each year?
Correct — interest expense = carrying amount × market rate, and since the carrying amount grows each year for a discount bond, so does interest expense.

04

Amortizing a Premium

The exact same mechanics as a discount, just running in reverse — the carrying amount falls toward face value instead of rising.

The mirror-image logic

A bond issued at a premium starts with a carrying amount above face value. Interest expense (carrying amount × market rate) is now less than the coupon payment — the difference reduces the carrying amount each year, until it reaches exactly face value at maturity.

The pattern, stated once and for all: a discount bond's carrying amount rises to face value; a premium bond's carrying amount falls to face value. In both cases, interest expense reflects the market rate at issuance, applied to a carrying amount that steadily converges toward face value — the coupon rate only ever determines the cash payment, never the reported expense.

Straight-line amortization — the simpler, cruder alternative

Instead of applying a constant rate to a changing carrying amount, the straight-line method simply divides the total discount or premium evenly across the bond's life. It's simpler, but doesn't tie interest expense to the actual effective rate — most standards prefer the effective interest method specifically because it does.

Check yourself
For a bond issued at a premium, how does interest expense compare to the coupon (cash) payment each year?
Correct — for a premium bond, the effective interest rate is below the coupon rate, so interest expense is less than the cash coupon payment each period.

05

The Fair Value Option

Companies can choose to report bonds at what they're actually worth today, instead of a steadily-amortizing historical figure — but almost nobody actually does.

Why amortized cost can mislead

Amortized cost reflects the market rate at issuance, frozen. As market rates move afterward, a bond's true fair value diverges from its amortized-cost carrying amount — when rates fall, a fixed-coupon bond's fair value rises, meaning the company's real economic liability may exceed its reported debt.

The fair value option's genuine mechanics: electing it means a decrease in the liability's fair value is reported as income (the company owes less), and an increase is a loss. But the portion of any fair value change caused specifically by the company's own credit risk deteriorating must be separated out and reported in OCI, not net income — isolating market-rate effects from the company's own creditworthiness.

Why so few companies actually elect it

As of the most recent data, the fair value option is used almost exclusively by financial-sector companies — Goldman Sachs among them — specifically because their financial assets are already fair-valued, and using amortized cost for their liabilities would create an artificial accounting mismatch, adding volatility that doesn't reflect real economic reality.

Check yourself
A company elects the fair value option, and the fair value of its bond liability decreases this period due to rising market rates. What is the effect on net income?
Correct — under the fair value option, a decrease in the liability's fair value (the company now owes less in economic terms) is recognized as income.

Why this matters for comparing companies: two companies with identical bonds can report meaningfully different carrying amounts and income volatility purely based on this election — always check the footnotes for which measurement basis is in use.

06

Derecognition of Debt

A bond leaves the balance sheet one of two ways — quietly at maturity, or with a bang if redeemed early.

Two ways a bond gets derecognized

At maturity: the discount or premium is by then fully amortized — carrying amount equals face value exactly. Bonds payable and cash both decrease by that same amount; a clean, gain/loss-free retirement.

Early redemption (calling or open-market repurchase): the difference between the cash paid to redeem and the bond's carrying amount at that moment is a genuine gain or loss, reported on the income statement.

Worked example — an early call

A company has a £10 million bond, carrying amount equal to face value, with 5 years remaining. It redeems the bonds at a call price of 103 (103% of face value) — costing £10.3 million in cash. Loss on redemption = £10 million carrying amount − £10.3 million cash paid = a £300,000 loss, recognized on the income statement.

On the cash flow statement (indirect method): any gain or loss on debt extinguishment gets removed from operating cash flow (it's a non-operating item), while the actual cash paid to redeem the bonds is classified as a financing outflow.

Check yourself
A company redeems bonds with a carrying amount of $5 million for $4.8 million in cash. What is the effect on the income statement?
Correct — paying less cash ($4.8M) than the carrying amount ($5M) to extinguish the debt produces a $200,000 gain.
