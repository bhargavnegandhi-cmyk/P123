01

Evaluating Past Financial Performance

Analyzing history isn't about nostalgia — it's the evidence base for judging management, and the foundation for looking forward.

The key questions an analyst asks

How and why have profitability, efficiency, liquidity, and solvency changed over the periods analyzed?

How does the company's performance compare with competitors in the same industry — and what explains any differences?

What is the company's business model and strategy, and how is that strategy actually reflected in its sales growth, efficiency, and profitability?

The evaluation addresses two different questions at once: not just what happened (the numbers), but why it happened (the causes) — and how that performance reflects a deliberate strategic choice, not just random variation.

Why this genuinely matters before forecasting anything

A company shifting from a low-margin, high-volume product line toward a high-margin, low-volume one will show declining revenue growth alongside rising gross margins — numbers that look contradictory in isolation, but make complete sense once you understand the underlying strategic shift. Understanding the "why" is what makes a forecast genuinely defensible, rather than a naive extrapolation of a trend that's about to change direction.

Check yourself
Why does an analyst need to understand the causes behind a company's past performance, not just the resulting numbers?
Correct — understanding why performance happened (strategic choices, not just outcomes) is what makes a forward-looking forecast genuinely well-grounded.

Data sources an analyst draws on: financial statements, investor relations materials, press releases, regulatory filings, industry surveys, consumer data, and firsthand research like site visits — rarely just the financial statements alone.

02

Projecting Future Financial Performance

Most professional sales forecasts don't start with the company at all — they start with the entire economy, then work down.

The top-down approach

Step 1: project industry sales based on their historical relationship to a macroeconomic indicator, like real GDP growth.

Step 2: project the specific company's market share, based on historical share and its competitive position.

Step 3: company sales = projected market share × projected industry sales.

From sales forecast to profit forecast
Forecasted profit = Forecasted sales × Forecasted profit margin

Historical operating margins are a more reliable starting point than net margins — net margin is distorted by financing and tax choices that don't reflect the core business.

When historical patterns genuinely stop being useful: for a start-up, a company mid-acquisition or divestiture, or one in a genuinely volatile industry, historical margins are far less reliable — the past simply isn't a good guide to what's coming.

Check yourself
Why do analysts often prefer using historical OPERATING profit margins over historical NET profit margins when forecasting?
Correct — operating margin reflects the core business more purely, while net margin is muddied by financing and tax decisions that can shift independent of operations.

These forecasts feed two very different uses: market-based (relative) valuation, where projected EPS gets multiplied by a projected P/E ratio — and credit analysis, where projected cash flow is checked against debt service obligations and covenant compliance.

03

Assessing Credit Risk

A creditor's upside is capped at the promised interest rate — which means credit analysis is almost entirely about the downside.

The asymmetry that defines credit analysis: if a company succeeds wildly, bondholders still only get their promised interest — no upside participation. If the company struggles, bondholders can lose everything. This asymmetry is why credit analysis focuses so intensely on downside scenarios and cash flow resilience, not growth potential.

Why credit analysts focus on cash flow, not accrual income

Debt payments are made in cash, not accrual earnings — so credit analysts lean heavily on operating cash flow, the cash a company genuinely generates internally and has available to service its creditors.

Real framework — Moody's four rating factors (Aerospace & Defense)

Leverage and coverage gets the heaviest weight — the level of debt relative to earnings and cash flow is the single most critical factor in whether a company can keep meeting its obligations.

Check yourself
Company 1 has Debt/EBITDA of 9.3 and EBIT/Interest of 5.7. Company 2 has Debt/EBITDA of 4.1 and EBIT/Interest of 8.2. Based solely on this, which company likely earns the higher credit rating?
Correct — Company 2's lower debt relative to EBITDA and higher interest coverage both point toward a stronger credit rating.

A genuine subtlety: rating factors and the specific metrics used vary meaningfully by industry — the framework that fits an airline won't be identical to one fitting a software company.

04

Screening for Potential Equity Investments

Five simple criteria, applied to 6,406 real companies, eliminated all but 17 — and the reason isn't quite what pure statistics would predict.

What screening does

Screening applies a set of criteria — typically financial ratios — to reduce a large universe of potential investments down to a smaller set with desired characteristics. It can serve as the direct basis for a portfolio, or as a preliminary filter before deeper research.

Real example — a 2018 stock screen of 6,406 US companies
Criterion% Passing
P/E < 1517.23%
Total liabilities/assets ≤ 0.90.95%
Operating income/sales > 054.78%
Dividend yield > 0.5%37.32%
All criteria simultaneously0.27% (17 stocks)

If the criteria were fully independent, only about 2 stocks would be expected to pass all five (multiplying the individual pass rates together). Seventeen actually passed — because the criteria are correlated, not independent. Dividend-paying status, for instance, tends to correlate positively with profitability.

Back-testing a screen carries the exact same bias risks covered in the Quant volume's Sampling module: survivorship bias (excluding failed companies inflates apparent performance), look-ahead bias (using restated data investors didn't actually have at the time), and data-snooping bias (repeatedly testing on the same historical data until something appears to "work").

Check yourself
A back-tested stock screen's database excludes companies that went bankrupt or were delisted during the test period. What bias does this most likely introduce?
Correct — excluding failed companies from the backtest database is a classic case of survivorship bias, inflating apparent historical performance.

05

A Framework for Analyst Adjustments & Inventory Adjustments

Comparing two companies fairly sometimes means un-doing their accounting choices first — starting with a question already familiar from Module 5.

Four questions to ask before adjusting anything

Importance: would this adjustment actually change the conclusions?

Body of standards: IFRS vs. US GAAP — where might that specifically matter here?

Methods: are the companies using genuinely different accounting methods?

Estimates: do they rely on meaningfully different assumptions?

Converting LIFO to a FIFO basis — straight from Module 5
FIFO inventory = LIFO inventory + LIFO reserve
FIFO COGS = LIFO COGS − change in LIFO reserve
Worked example — comparing current ratios across standards

LP Tech reports under LIFO with current assets of $820.2M and current liabilities of $218.1M — a current ratio of 3.76. A comparable company reports under IFRS (FIFO only, since IFRS prohibits LIFO). To compare fairly, the analyst must first add LP Tech's disclosed LIFO reserve back into its inventory (and thus current assets) before computing a current ratio that's genuinely comparable to the FIFO-based competitor.

Check yourself
An analyst is comparing a US company using LIFO with a European competitor using FIFO. What must the analyst do to make their current ratios comparable?
Correct — adding the LIFO reserve back restates LIFO inventory onto a FIFO-equivalent basis, enabling a genuinely fair comparison.

Why this framework applies to the balance sheet first: financial statements are interrelated, so a balance sheet adjustment (like inventory) automatically ripples into the income statement (cost of goods sold) and back into retained earnings — one adjustment, three statements affected.

06

Adjustments Related to Property, Plant & Equipment

A handful of simple ratios, built from disclosures every company already provides, reveal how old a company's asset base really is.

Four revealing PP&E ratios

Accumulated depreciation ÷ gross PP&E: how much of the asset base's useful life has already passed.

Accumulated depreciation ÷ depreciation expense: the average age, in years, of the asset base.

Net PP&E ÷ depreciation expense: roughly how many years of useful life remain.

Capex ÷ (gross PP&E + capex): what share of the asset base is currently being renewed.

Why this matters even without a common depreciation method

Since companies rarely disclose enough detail to make precise, item-by-item depreciation adjustments, these ratios instead give a genuinely useful qualitative signal — two competitors in the same industry with very different "average asset age" ratios likely have different capital investment strategies worth investigating further, even without perfectly comparable accounting.

Check yourself
Company A has accumulated depreciation of $800M and gross PP&E of $1,000M. Company B has accumulated depreciation of $200M and gross PP&E of $1,000M. Which company's asset base is, on average, older?
Correct — Company A's ratio (800/1,000 = 80%) indicates a much older, more depreciated asset base than Company B's (200/1,000 = 20%).

A genuine limitation to keep in mind: PP&E typically pools assets with very different depreciable lives and salvage values, so these ratios reflect broad averages across the whole asset base, not any single asset's specific age.

07

Adjustments Related to Goodwill

Two economically identical companies can show wildly different balance sheets, purely based on whether they grew by acquisition or built everything themselves.

What goodwill actually represents

When one company acquires another for more than the fair value of its identifiable net assets, the excess is recorded as goodwill — an asset representing whatever the acquirer paid above the target's identifiable value (brands, technology, customer relationships, and the like).

Worked example

ParentCo buys TargetCo for $400 million; TargetCo's identifiable net assets (including a recognized brand) are worth $300 million. ParentCo records $300 million of identifiable assets plus $100 million of goodwill — the premium paid above identifiable value.

The genuine comparability problem

Two companies with identical underlying economic value — same brand strength, same trained workforce, same proprietary technology — can show completely different balance sheets. The company that grew by acquisition capitalizes that value as goodwill. The company that grew internally expensed its advertising, training, and R&D as incurred — meaning that identical economic value never appears on its balance sheet at all. Ratios like ROA and market-value-to-book-value will differ sharply between the two, despite genuinely equal underlying value.

Check yourself
Two companies have identical economic value, but Company X grew by acquisition while Company Y grew entirely through internal investment. Why will their reported ROA likely differ?
Correct — Company X's larger recorded asset base (including goodwill) versus Company Y's unrecorded internally-generated value changes the ROA denominator, even with identical underlying economics.

The practical analyst response: when comparing an acquisitive company to an organically-grown one, consider excluding goodwill from both the numerator (income, if any impairment occurred) and denominator (total assets) of return ratios, to get a cleaner apples-to-apples comparison.
