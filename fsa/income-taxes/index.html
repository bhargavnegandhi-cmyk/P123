CFA Program · Prerequisite Financial Statement Analysis · Module 7

Every company keeps two sets of books — legally, and for a genuinely good reason.

The rules for reporting profit to shareholders and the rules for reporting profit to tax authorities aren't the same rulebook — and they were never meant to be. This module is about the gap between them: why it exists, when it reverses itself, and how to read the deferred tax assets and liabilities that gap leaves behind on the balance sheet.

01

Accounting Income vs. Taxable Income

Four terms that sound almost interchangeable, but mean genuinely distinct things.

The four key terms

Accounting (pre-tax) income: profit reported to shareholders, following accounting standards like IFRS or US GAAP.

Taxable income: profit as calculated under tax law, used to determine taxes actually owed.

Income tax expense: the total tax cost reported on the income statement — current tax payable plus (or minus) the change in deferred taxes.

Taxes payable: the actual amount currently owed to tax authorities, based on taxable income.

Why the two income figures genuinely differ: accounting standards and tax law exist for different purposes — one aims to fairly represent economic performance to investors, the other to fund government revenue with its own policy priorities (encouraging investment, discouraging certain behavior). They were never designed to produce the same number.

Check yourself
Which figure represents the amount a company actually owes tax authorities for the current period?
Correct — taxes payable is the actual current obligation to tax authorities, calculated from taxable income under tax law, distinct from the income tax expense reported on the income statement.

Where deferred taxes come from: whenever income tax expense (based on accounting income) differs from taxes payable (based on taxable income) in a given year, the difference creates a deferred tax asset or liability — the subject of the rest of this module.

02

Determining the Tax Base of an Asset

Two different numbers can describe the exact same asset — its accounting carrying amount, and its tax base — and the gap between them is where deferred taxes are born.

The definition

The tax base of an asset is the amount that will be deductible for tax purposes in future periods, as the asset's economic benefits are realized. It's genuinely distinct from the carrying amount, which follows accounting rules.

Flagship worked example — Entiguan Sports

Entiguan Sports capitalizes €3,000,000 of development costs, amortizing €500,000 this year (accounting basis). Tax law allows 25% amortization per year.

Carrying amount = 3,000,000 − 500,000 = €2,500,000. Tax base = 3,000,000 − (25% × 3,000,000) = €2,250,000. The €250,000 gap is a temporary difference — since the carrying amount exceeds the tax base, this creates a deferred tax liability.

The core rule for assets
Carrying amount > Tax base → Deferred Tax Liability
Carrying amount < Tax base → Deferred Tax Asset
Check yourself
An asset's carrying amount for accounting purposes is $80,000, while its tax base is $95,000. What does this difference create?
Correct — when carrying amount is less than tax base for an asset, this creates a deferred tax asset.

03

Determining the Tax Base of a Liability

Liabilities work through the identical logic as assets — just mirrored.

The definition

The tax base of a liability is its carrying amount minus any amount that will be deductible for tax purposes in the future.

Worked example — rent received in advance

Entiguan Sports receives €10,000,000 of rent in advance. For accounting purposes, this is deferred (a liability) until earned. But tax law taxes cash received immediately, regardless of when it's earned.

Carrying amount = €10,000,000 (the full liability). Tax base = carrying amount − amount already taxed = 10,000,000 − 10,000,000 = €0. Since the carrying amount of the liability exceeds its tax base, this creates a deferred tax asset — the company has effectively prepaid tax on income it hasn't recognized for accounting purposes yet.

The core rule for liabilities (mirrored from assets)
Carrying amount > Tax base → Deferred Tax Asset
Carrying amount < Tax base → Deferred Tax Liability
Check yourself
A company's warranty liability has a carrying amount of $200,000, but tax law only allows a deduction once the warranty repair is actually performed, giving it a tax base of $0. What does this create?
Correct — for a liability, carrying amount exceeding tax base creates a deferred tax asset, since the company will get a future tax deduction it hasn't yet recognized for accounting purposes.

04

Temporary vs. Permanent Differences

One type of gap between accounting and tax income eventually closes on its own. The other never does — and only one of them creates a deferred tax item at all.

The distinction

Temporary difference: a difference expected to reverse in a future period — the carrying amount and tax base will eventually converge. Creates a deferred tax asset or liability.

Permanent difference: a difference that will never reverse. Creates no deferred tax item at all — it simply makes the effective tax rate differ from the statutory rate, permanently.

Worked example — donations

Entiguan Sports expenses €100,000 of donations on its income statement, but tax law never allows donations as a deduction. Carrying amount = €0, tax base = €0 — no temporary difference at all, since there's nothing to eventually reverse. This is a permanent difference: the donation expense simply never gets a tax benefit, ever.

Check yourself
A jurisdiction permanently disallows entertainment expenses as a tax deduction, though they're fully expensed for accounting purposes. What effect does this have?
Correct — since this difference will never reverse, it's permanent, and creates no deferred tax asset or liability, though it does affect the effective tax rate.

Quick test: ask "will this gap eventually close by itself, as the asset is used up or the liability settled?" If yes, temporary. If the gap is permanent by its very nature (like a non-deductible expense category), it's permanent — and it never touches deferred taxes.

05

The Valuation Allowance

A deferred tax asset is only genuinely valuable if the company expects enough future taxable profit to actually use it.

What it is

Under US GAAP, a deferred tax asset is initially recognized in full, then reduced by a valuation allowance if it's more likely than not that some or all of it won't actually be realized — typically because future taxable profits are genuinely in doubt.

What a growing valuation allowance signals

A history of tax losses is a red flag — it may indicate the company genuinely can't be expected to generate enough future taxable income to use its deferred tax assets. Establishing a valuation allowance reduces both the deferred tax asset and reported income in the period it's established — a real hit to earnings, not just a balance sheet reclassification.

Why analysts scrutinize valuation allowance reversals

If circumstances improve, a valuation allowance can be reversed — which increases the deferred tax asset and boosts reported operating income in that period. Because this judgment is genuinely subjective, a sudden, large reversal right when a company needs an earnings boost deserves real scrutiny — it may reflect real improved prospects, or it may be a convenient lever for managing reported earnings.

Check yourself
A company with a multi-year history of tax losses records a valuation allowance against its deferred tax asset. What is the effect on reported income in the period the allowance is established?
Correct — establishing a valuation allowance reduces the deferred tax asset and reported income in that same period.

Factors used to judge realizability: the probability of sufficient future taxable profits, whether past losses stemmed from circumstances unlikely to repeat, and available tax planning opportunities that could generate future profits.

06

Changes in Income Tax Rates

Deferred tax balances aren't frozen at the rate that created them — they get revalued the instant tax law changes.

The rule
Deferred taxes are measured at the tax rate expected to apply when the asset is realized or the liability settled

When a tax rate changes, every existing deferred tax balance must be revalued — using the new rate, not the rate in effect when the temporary difference originated. A rate cut reduces the value of existing deferred tax liabilities (a genuine boost to reported income); a rate increase does the opposite, raising both DTAs and DTLs.

Worked example

A company has a $500,000 deferred tax liability recorded at a 30% tax rate (from a $1,666,667 temporary difference). The government cuts the corporate tax rate to 21%. The DTL must be revalued: $1,666,667 × 21% = $350,000 — a $150,000 reduction, recognized as a one-time boost to income tax expense (i.e., a reduction in the expense) in the period the rate change is enacted.

Check yourself
A government enacts a significant corporate tax rate increase. What is the most likely effect on a company's existing deferred tax liabilities?
Correct — a tax rate increase requires revaluing existing deferred tax liabilities upward, using the new expected settlement rate.

07

Taxes Charged Directly to Equity

If the item that caused a deferred tax bypassed the income statement, the resulting tax should bypass it too — consistency, not coincidence.

The consistency principle

When an item that creates a deferred tax liability is itself taken directly to equity (bypassing net income), the related deferred tax should follow it directly to equity too — not flow through the income statement.

Common examples

Revaluation of property, plant, and equipment under IFRS's revaluation model (Module 6) is the classic case — since the revaluation surplus itself goes to OCI/equity, not net income, the associated deferred tax liability follows it directly into equity as well. Other examples include certain fair value changes on long-term investments and foreign currency translation adjustments.

This ties the whole volume together: Module 2's comprehensive income, Module 3's AOCI, Module 6's revaluation surplus, and this module's deferred taxes are all the same underlying idea — some genuine gains and losses simply never touch net income, and the tax consequences of those items follow the same path.

Check yourself
A company revalues a building upward under IFRS, recognizing the gain in other comprehensive income. Where should the associated deferred tax liability be recognized?
Correct — since the revaluation gain itself bypasses net income, the associated deferred tax liability should also be recognized directly in equity, for consistency.

This is exactly why an analyst reviewing a company's deferred tax footnote should always check whether a deferred tax movement flowed through net income or bypassed it entirely — the two have very different implications for evaluating reported earnings quality.
