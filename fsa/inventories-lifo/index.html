The same accounting choice that saves a company real money in taxes can also mislead an analyst reading its margins.

Part 1 covered how inventory gets measured. This half is about what happens next — specifically, what LIFO does to every major number on the financial statements when costs are genuinely rising (the far more common real-world case), and how to reverse-engineer a LIFO company's figures back onto a comparable FIFO basis using nothing but a single footnote disclosure.

04

Rising Costs — How LIFO and FIFO Diverge

In the far more common real-world case of rising costs, LIFO and FIFO pull every major statement and ratio in genuinely opposite directions.

When inventory costs are rising, LIFO produces:

Income statement: higher cost of sales → lower gross profit, operating profit, and net income.

Balance sheet: lower ending inventory → lower working capital, total assets, and equity.

Cash flow: lower taxable income → lower taxes paid → higher operating cash flow, the one place LIFO actually looks "better."

Why a company would deliberately choose LIFO

The tax savings are the entire motivation. Lower reported taxable income under LIFO genuinely means paying less tax in cash today — and since company value is ultimately about the present value of future cash flows, that tax deferral has real economic value, even though it makes reported profit look worse.

Check yourself
During a period of rising inventory costs, a company switches from FIFO to LIFO. What happens to its reported net operating cash flow, all else equal?
Correct — LIFO's higher cost of sales lowers taxable income and taxes paid, which actually increases operating cash flow, even as reported net income falls.

05

LIFO Reserve & LIFO-to-FIFO Conversion

One required disclosure lets an analyst reconstruct exactly what a LIFO company's numbers would have looked like under FIFO.

LIFO reserve
LIFO Reserve = FIFO Inventory − LIFO Inventory

US GAAP requires companies using LIFO to disclose this figure, in a note or directly on the balance sheet.

The two-step conversion

Inventory: FIFO inventory = LIFO inventory (reported) + LIFO reserve.

Cost of sales: FIFO cost of sales = LIFO cost of sales (reported) − the increase in the LIFO reserve during the period (add back a decrease, if the reserve fell).

Why this matters so much for comparability

A US company using LIFO and a European competitor using FIFO (IFRS prohibits LIFO entirely) will show genuinely different gross margins purely from this accounting choice — even with economically identical operations. The LIFO reserve conversion is exactly the tool that lets an analyst put both companies on the same footing before comparing them.

Check yourself
A company reports LIFO inventory of $40 million and discloses a LIFO reserve of $12 million. What would its inventory be if it had used FIFO instead?
Correct — FIFO inventory = LIFO inventory + LIFO reserve = $40M + $12M = $52 million.

06

LIFO Liquidation

Selling more than you buy digs into old, cheap inventory layers — creating a one-time profit boost that's genuinely a warning sign, not good news.

What causes it

When units sold exceed units purchased or produced in a period, a LIFO company draws down into older, lower-cost inventory layers that would normally stay untouched. If costs have been rising, this produces an inventory-related jump in gross profit — but purely because old, cheap costs are being matched against current, higher sale prices.

Why this is a genuine red flag, not a real improvement

The profit boost from LIFO liquidation is a one-time, unsustainable event — it can't recur once those old layers are gone. Worse, management can potentially manipulate reported earnings around a weak quarter by deliberately running down inventory to trigger this effect. A declining LIFO reserve from one period to the next is the specific signal analysts watch for to catch this.

Outside example

A manufacturer facing a supplier labor strike is forced to sell down existing inventory faster than it can restock. If costs have risen steadily for years, the oldest LIFO layers being liquidated might have been recorded at half today's cost — producing an artificially inflated gross margin that has nothing to do with the business's actual current profitability.

Check yourself
A LIFO company's reserve declined significantly from the prior year, and inventory unit costs have been rising for years. What does this most likely indicate?
Correct — a declining LIFO reserve, alongside historically rising costs, is the classic signal of a LIFO liquidation and its associated one-time profit boost.

The practical takeaway: always check the LIFO reserve footnote year over year before trusting a reported gross margin improvement at a LIFO company — it might be real operating improvement, or it might just be inventory liquidation.

07

Lower of Cost and Net Realisable Value

Inventory is never allowed to sit on the books at more than it could actually be sold for — the accounting version of "don't kid yourself."

The rule and the key term

IFRS rule: inventory is carried at the lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV).

Net realisable value: the estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business, minus the estimated costs to complete and sell the item.

Worked example — a write-down

A batch of inventory cost 4.20 CHF/kg to produce, but due to obsolescence, its net realisable value has fallen to 3.95 CHF/kg. The inventory must be written down by 0.25 CHF/kg — recognized as an expense (typically within cost of sales) on the income statement, with the balance sheet inventory value reduced to match the lower NRV.

A genuine IFRS vs. US GAAP difference

Under IFRS, if inventory value recovers later, the write-down can be reversed (limited to the original write-down amount). Under US GAAP, once written down, that value is a new cost basis — reversal is prohibited entirely, even if the inventory's value genuinely recovers.

Check yourself
Inventory with a cost of $80,000 has a net realisable value of $65,000. Under IFRS, what is the required accounting treatment?
Correct — inventory must be written down to the lower of cost and NRV, recognizing the $15,000 difference as an expense.
