CFA Program · Prerequisite Financial Statement Analysis · Module 2

Deciding when a sale actually "counts" turns out to be a genuinely hard problem.

Revenue minus expenses equals net income — the equation itself takes five words. Getting the timing and amount of each side right, consistently, across every industry from software subscriptions to twenty-year construction contracts, is what this entire module is actually about. This is the reading analysts scrutinize more than any other, because earnings drive valuations more than any other single number.

01

What the Income Statement Reports

Three different names, one basic equation, and a genuinely intense level of scrutiny from every corner of the market.

The basic equation
Revenue − Expenses = Net Income

You'll also see the income statement called the statement of operations, the statement of earnings, or the profit and loss (P&L) statement — all the same thing. It reports results over a period of time (a quarter, a year) — unlike the balance sheet, which is a snapshot at one instant.

Why analysts obsess over this specific statement

Equity analysts care because markets reward high-earnings-growth companies with premium valuations, and most valuation models are built directly on earnings estimates. Fixed-income analysts scrutinize it for a company's ability to keep making debt payments through a full business cycle. Corporate announcements emphasize earnings more than any other single reported figure.

Check yourself
Which statement below correctly distinguishes the income statement from the balance sheet?
Correct — the income statement summarizes performance over a period, while the balance sheet captures position at a single moment.

Same statement, different names: statement of operations, statement of earnings, P&L statement, and income statement all refer to the identical report — don't let the naming variation across companies and textbooks throw you off.

02

The Five-Step Revenue Recognition Model

One converged global standard (IFRS 15 / ASC 606) governs when and how much revenue a company gets to report — five steps, applied to every contract.

The five steps

1. Identify the contract(s) with a customer.

2. Identify the separate performance obligations — the distinct promises within the contract.

3. Determine the transaction price — what the seller expects to receive.

4. Allocate the transaction price to each performance obligation.

5. Recognize revenue when (or as) each performance obligation is satisfied.

Worked example — Builder Co.'s long-term construction contract

Builder Co. contracts to construct a commercial building for $1,000,000, with expected total costs of $700,000. In year one, it incurs $420,000 of costs. Since the building is one integrated deliverable (not separable pre-construction, plumbing, wiring, etc. as distinct obligations), it's treated as a single performance obligation, satisfied over time.

Progress is measured as costs incurred ÷ total expected costs = $420,000/$700,000 = 60%. Revenue recognized in year one = 60% × $1,000,000 = $600,000 — this is the same result the old "percentage-of-completion" method would have produced, just described in the new standard's language.

Check yourself
Builder Co. incurs $420,000 in costs in Year 1, and $700,000 total costs are expected. It's Year 2, and it now expects $150,000 more revenue plus a $200,000 completion bonus, raising total revenue to $1,350,000. Costs incurred are still $420,000, but total expected costs rise to $820,000. What is the new percentage of completion?
Correct — percentage of completion = costs incurred / total expected costs = 420,000/820,000 = 51.2%, using the updated cost estimate after the contract modification.

03

Principal vs. Agent — Gross vs. Net Revenue

The exact same $100 sale can legitimately be reported as $100 of revenue by one company, and $10 by another — depending on who's actually taking the risk.

The distinction

Principal: primarily responsible for fulfilling the contract, bears inventory and credit risk, has discretion in setting price. Reports revenue gross — the full sale amount.

Agent: arranges for another party to provide the goods or service, doesn't bear the same risks, earns a commission. Reports revenue net — just the commission.

Worked example — a marketplace website

A company operates a website connecting customers with suppliers. Customers pay in advance and orders are non-refundable, but suppliers ship directly to customers, and the company earns a 10% commission. Because the company isn't responsible for fulfillment, doesn't take inventory or credit risk, and doesn't control pricing — it's acting as an agent. On a $500 order, it reports only $50 of revenue (its commission), not the full $500.

Check yourself
A retailer buys inventory outright, sets its own prices, and bears the risk if goods don't sell. When it sells $200 of goods, how much revenue should it report?
Correct — bearing inventory risk and controlling price are hallmarks of a principal, who reports the full (gross) sale amount as revenue.

Why this matters for comparing companies: two companies with identical underlying economics can show wildly different revenue figures purely based on principal-vs-agent classification — a critical adjustment analysts need to check before comparing "revenue growth" across marketplace-style businesses.

04

Expense Recognition: Doubtful Accounts & Warranties

You have to guess how much of today's sale will never actually get paid — and record that guess as an expense today, not later.

The wrong way — waiting for certainty

Waiting until a specific customer actually defaults before recording any loss (the "direct write-off method") sounds simpler, but it violates the matching principle — the expense would land in a completely different period from the revenue it relates to. This approach generally isn't accepted under GAAP or IFRS.

The required approach — estimate upfront

Doubtful accounts: at the moment revenue is recognized, the company must also estimate — based on historical experience — how much of it will ultimately go uncollected, and record that as an expense immediately.

Warranties: the same logic applies — estimate future warranty repair/replacement costs at the time of sale, record the expense then, and true up the estimate against actual experience over the warranty's life.

Why this is really just the matching principle again

Both doubtful accounts and warranty expense are the exact same idea from Module 00's matching principle, applied to two specific, genuinely uncertain future costs. The revenue is certain today; the associated cost isn't — but accrual accounting still demands a best estimate be recorded in the same period as the revenue, not whenever the uncertainty eventually resolves.

Check yourself
A company sells $500,000 of goods on credit this quarter and, based on historical experience, expects 2% to ultimately be uncollectible. When should this expected loss be recorded?
Correct — the matching principle requires the estimated uncollectible amount to be recorded in the same period as the related revenue.

Both estimates get revisited and adjusted over time as actual experience comes in — an estimate made today isn't locked in forever; it's refined as reality unfolds.

05

Expense Recognition: Depreciation & Amortization

The matching principle, applied to assets that stick around for years instead of days.

Two words, same idea, different asset types

Depreciation: the systematic allocation of a physical long-lived asset's cost (plant, equipment) over its useful life. Land is never depreciated.

Amortization: the identical idea applied to intangible assets with a finite useful life (a patent with a set expiration, an acquired mailing list). Intangibles with an indefinite life (like goodwill) are never amortized — instead, they're tested annually for impairment.

Straight-line depreciation
Annual depreciation = (Cost − Residual value) / Useful life
Two estimates drive the whole calculation

Both the useful life and the residual (salvage) value are estimates management has to make upfront — and both are genuinely sensitive levers. A longer assumed useful life or a higher residual value both shrink annual depreciation expense, boosting reported profit — without changing anything about the asset's actual physical wear.

Cost model vs. revaluation model (IFRS only): under the cost model, an asset is carried at cost minus accumulated depreciation. Under the revaluation model — permitted under IFRS but NOT under US GAAP, and rarely used in practice — the asset is instead carried at fair value.

Check yourself
A company raises its estimate of an asset's useful life from 5 years to 10 years, with no other changes. What happens to annual depreciation expense?
Correct — spreading the same depreciable cost over a longer useful life reduces the annual expense, all else equal.

06

Operating vs. Non-Operating Items

The same dollar of income can mean something very different depending on which bucket it falls into.

The distinction

Operating items: arise from a company's core, central business activities — producing and delivering the goods or services it actually exists to sell.

Non-operating items: arise from peripheral activities — most commonly interest expense/income and gains or losses from investing activities, for a typical non-financial company.

The classification genuinely depends on the business. Interest income and expense are non-operating for a typical manufacturer — but for a bank or insurance company, interest IS the core operating activity. There's no universal rule; it depends entirely on what the company is actually in business to do.

Why separating these matters for analysis

A manufacturer's future performance depends on demand for its products and its cost structure — that's the operating story. Its non-operating financing expense depends instead on its capital structure and borrowing costs — a completely different driver. Blending the two together obscures which lever is actually driving results.

Check yourself
For a typical non-financial manufacturing company, how is interest expense on its debt usually classified?
Correct — for a typical manufacturer, interest expense relates to financing decisions, not core operations, so it's classified as non-operating.

A genuine wrinkle: a company's income statement classification of interest isn't always consistent with how the same item appears on the cash flow statement — IFRS actually allows some flexibility here that US GAAP does not.

07

Common-Size Analysis of the Income Statement

Strip away company size entirely, and suddenly a $2 million company can be compared fairly against a $10 million one.

Vertical common-size analysis
Every line item ÷ Revenue, expressed as a percentage
Worked example — three companies, one comparison

Company A and B both have $10 million in sales; Company C has only $2 million. Company A's operating profit is $2 million, Company B's is $1.5 million, and Company C's is just $400,000 — in raw dollars, C looks like the weakest performer by far.

Once converted to common-size percentages, though: Company C's operating margin is 20% ($400,000/$2,000,000) — identical to Company A's, and actually better than Company B's 15% ($1,500,000/$10,000,000). Size was hiding C's genuinely stronger operating efficiency.

Check yourself
Why is common-size analysis specifically useful for comparing companies of very different sizes?
Correct — by standardizing every line item as a percentage of revenue, common-size analysis makes companies of vastly different absolute sizes genuinely comparable.

08

Comprehensive Income

Some real gains and losses never touch net income at all — but they still have to show up somewhere, or the balance sheet stops making sense.

Comprehensive income
Net Income + Other Comprehensive Income (OCI)
Four common types of other comprehensive income

1. Foreign currency translation adjustments (consolidating foreign subsidiaries)

2. Unrealized gains/losses on derivatives used as hedges

3. Unrealized holding gains/losses on certain investment securities

4. Certain unrecognized costs from defined-benefit pension plans

Worked example — finding the "missing" OCI

Beginning shareholders' equity is €110 million. Net income for the year is €10 million, dividends paid are €2 million, and there's no new stock issued or repurchased. If ending equity is actually €123 million, what happened to the extra €5 million?

Expected ending equity (net income and dividends only) = €110 + €10 − €2 = €118 million. Actual ending equity is €123 million — the €5 million gap (€123 − €118) is other comprehensive income, a real change in equity that bypassed the income statement entirely.

Check yourself
A company holds available-for-sale debt securities whose value rises during the year, but it hasn't sold them. Where does this unrealized gain typically appear?
Correct — unrealized gains on available-for-sale securities typically flow through other comprehensive income rather than net income, while still affecting shareholders' equity.
