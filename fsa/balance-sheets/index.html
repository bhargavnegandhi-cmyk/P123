A photograph, not a valuation — and the difference matters more than it sounds.

The income statement covers a stretch of time. The balance sheet is the opposite: everything a company owns and owes, frozen at one exact instant. This module goes past the accounting equation you already know from Module 00, into how real companies like SAP, Apple, and HSBC actually organize that snapshot — and why the "equity" number on it was never meant to tell you what the company is actually worth.

01

What the Balance Sheet Reports

Also called the statement of financial position — and genuinely not a market valuation of the company, even though it's tempting to read it that way.

Two ways to read the same equation

A = L + E can be read as: the left side shows the resources a company controls; the right side shows how those resources were financed — by owners (equity) or by others (liabilities). Every asset on the left has a financing story on the right.

Why balance sheet equity ≠ market value

1. It's a mixed measurement model — some items sit at historical cost, others at fair value, within the same statement.

2. Even fair-value items go stale immediately — they reflect value as of the balance sheet date, which can move the very next day.

3. Genuine value drivers are missing entirely — a company's reputation, its management team's skill, its brand strength — none of it sits on the balance sheet, even though all of it drives future cash flows.

Check yourself
Why shouldn't an analyst treat a company's balance sheet equity as equivalent to its market value?
Correct — mixed measurement bases, a single point-in-time snapshot, and the exclusion of unrecorded value drivers all mean book equity and market value are genuinely different things.

Naming note: IFRS calls this the "statement of financial position." US GAAP uses "balance sheet" and "statement of financial position" interchangeably. Same report, different preferred names.

02

Current vs. Non-Current Classification

One year, or one operating cycle — whichever is longer. That's the entire dividing line between "current" and everything else.

The classification rule

Current assets: expected to be sold, used up, or converted to cash within one year or one operating cycle, whichever is greater.

Current liabilities: expected to be settled within that same window, or held primarily for trading, or the entity lacks an unconditional right to defer settlement beyond a year.

Why a lumber company's 18-month-old inventory can still be "current"

A company's operating cycle is the average time from acquiring inventory to collecting cash from its sale. Tobacco, distillery, and lumber businesses often have operating cycles well over a year — their inventory sits in a warehouse aging for a long time by design. Even inventory held 18 months still counts as a current asset, because it's genuinely expected to sell within that industry's normal operating cycle, not within a rigid calendar year.

Working capital
Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities
Check yourself
A distillery holds whiskey inventory that ages in barrels for 3 years before being sold, well beyond a calendar year. Should this inventory be classified as a current or non-current asset?
Correct — the one-year-or-operating-cycle rule uses whichever is greater, so inventory genuinely expected to sell within a longer normal operating cycle still counts as current.

03

Liquidity-Based Presentation

For a bank, "current vs. non-current" barely means anything — so IFRS lets certain companies present their balance sheet a completely different way.

Most companies use a classified balance sheet — current items grouped separately from non-current items. But under IFRS, a company can instead use a liquidity-based presentation, listing every asset and liability in order of liquidity, when that's more relevant and reliable.

Real example — HSBC's balance sheet

HSBC, a global bank, starts its asset listing with cash and balances at central banks — the most liquid item possible — and works down toward less liquid items like interests in associates and joint ventures near the bottom. There's no "current assets" subtotal at all, because for a bank, almost every asset and liability is inherently tied to trading, lending, and interest-rate exposure — the current/non-current distinction just isn't as meaningful as it is for a manufacturer.

Check yourself
Why might a global bank like HSBC use a liquidity-based balance sheet presentation instead of a classified (current/non-current) one?
Correct — for entities like banks, ordering assets and liabilities by liquidity provides more relevant, reliable information than a current/non-current split.

Both SAP and Apple, in contrast, use classified balance sheets — current assets and liabilities presented as their own distinct subtotal, ahead of everything else.

04

Current Assets — Cash, Marketable Securities & Receivables

Four specific line items are required to be shown separately, whenever material — everything else is company discretion.

The required current-asset categories

Cash and cash equivalents: cash plus highly liquid investments with original maturities of three months or less — Treasury bills, commercial paper, money market funds.

Trade and other receivables: amounts owed to the company by its customers.

Inventories: goods held for sale or used in production.

Financial assets with short maturities: short-term marketable securities.

Real example — Apple's current asset mix

Apple's FY2017 balance sheet shows $20.3B cash and equivalents, $53.9B short-term marketable securities, $17.9B accounts receivable, just $4.9B inventories (notably lean for a hardware company — a deliberate supply-chain efficiency), and $17.8B "vendor non-trade receivables" — a line item specific to Apple's unusual arrangement of supplying components to manufacturing partners.

Cash equivalents have a hard cutoff: only investments with original maturities of three months or less qualify. Something maturing in four months, however safe, doesn't belong in "cash and cash equivalents" — it belongs elsewhere, typically short-term investments.

Check yourself
A company holds a US Treasury bill with an original maturity of 2 months. How should this typically be classified?
Correct — with an original maturity under three months, this Treasury bill qualifies as a cash equivalent.

05

Current Liabilities — Payables, Financial Liabilities & Deferred Income

Everything a company owes and expects to settle soon, split into a few genuinely distinct types.

Common types of current liabilities

Trade payables (accounts payable): unpaid amounts owed to vendors for purchases on credit — the flip side of another company's receivables.

Financial liabilities: bank loans, notes payable, commercial paper due within the operating cycle — plus the current portion of any long-term debt.

Accrued expenses: costs incurred but not yet paid or invoiced.

Deferred (unearned) revenue: cash already received for goods or services not yet delivered.

Why trade payables trends matter to analysts

A meaningful shift in accounts payable relative to purchase volume can signal a changing relationship with suppliers — either a company negotiating better payment terms (a good sign), or suppliers tightening credit because they're worried about getting paid (a warning sign). The direction alone doesn't tell you which; context does.

Check yourself
A software company receives a full year's subscription payment upfront but hasn't yet delivered 11 of the 12 months of service. How should the unearned portion be classified?
Correct — cash received before the related service is delivered creates a liability (deferred revenue), recognized as revenue only as the service is actually delivered.

Watch for the "current portion of long-term debt": even a 10-year bond gets partially reclassified as a current liability once its next principal payment falls within the coming year — a detail that trips up analysts who assume "long-term debt" always stays entirely non-current.

06

Components of Shareholders' Equity

"Equity" is really six different things added together, and real companies rarely show all six with the same labels.

The six components

1. Contributed capital: what owners directly paid into the company for shares.

2. Preferred shares: classified as equity or as a liability based on their actual characteristics, not just their label — mandatory redemption pushes them toward liability treatment.

3. Treasury shares: the company's own previously-issued shares, repurchased and held (not cancelled) — reduces total equity, and these shares carry no vote and receive no dividend.

4. Retained earnings: cumulative net income earned over the company's life, minus all dividends ever paid.

5. Accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI): the cumulative balance of every year's OCI from Module 2 — the running total that OCI feeds into.

6. Non-controlling (minority) interest: the slice of a consolidated subsidiary's equity that belongs to outside shareholders, not the parent company.

Real example — SAP vs. Apple's equity sections

SAP shows all six components explicitly: €1,229M issued capital, €1,591M treasury shares, €24,794M retained earnings, €508M other comprehensive income, plus a non-controlling interest of €31M. Apple's equity section, by contrast, shows just three line items — common stock, retained earnings, and AOCI — because Apple cancels repurchased shares rather than holding them as treasury stock, and has no meaningful non-controlling interests to report.

Check yourself
A company repurchases its own shares and holds them, rather than cancelling them. What is this called, and what's its effect on total equity?
Correct — shares repurchased and held (not cancelled) become treasury shares, reducing total shareholders' equity by the repurchase cost.
