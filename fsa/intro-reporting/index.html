Module 00 built the mechanics — the accounting equation, accrual accounting, debits and credits. This module steps back and asks who actually writes the rulebook those mechanics follow, what "useful" financial information even means, and the formal vocabulary — elements, recognition, measurement — that every later module in this volume will lean on constantly.

01

Standard-Setters vs. Regulators

One group writes the rules. A completely different group has the legal power to enforce them.

Two different jobs, easy to confuse

Standard-setting bodies — like the IASB and FASB — are private-sector, self-regulated organizations of accountants, auditors, and academics who actually write accounting standards. They have no legal power on their own.

Regulatory authorities — like the SEC in the US, or similar bodies elsewhere — have the legal authority to require companies to actually follow those standards, and to enforce compliance within their jurisdiction.

Why this distinction genuinely matters

Without a regulator's recognition, a standard-setting body's rules are just a well-written suggestion. The IASB writes IFRS; it's the individual countries' regulators who decide whether companies within their borders are legally required to follow it. Regulators generally retain the power to overrule the standard-setters within their own jurisdiction, even though this is rare in practice.

The two most influential standard-setters

IASB (International Accounting Standards Board): issues IFRS, used by the vast majority of countries globally.

FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board): issues US GAAP, used in the United States, recognized as authoritative by the SEC.

Check yourself
Which of these best describes the relationship between the IASB and national regulatory authorities?
Correct — standard-setting bodies like the IASB draft the rules, but it's the regulatory authorities in each jurisdiction that give those rules actual legal force.

02

The Objective of Financial Reporting

Every rule in every accounting standard ultimately traces back to a single sentence.

The IASB's stated objective
Provide financial information useful to current and potential resource providers in making decisions

"Resource providers" mainly means investors and lenders — the people deciding whether to give the company money, in exchange for equity or debt. Everything else in the conceptual framework — every qualitative characteristic, every measurement rule — exists in service of that one goal.

What users actually need to know

Financial position: what resources (assets) the company has, and what it owes (liabilities).

Financial performance: how and why that position changed over the period.

Cash position: where cash came from, and where it went.

Check yourself
According to the IASB's Conceptual Framework, who are the primary intended users of financial reports?
Correct — the framework centers on resource providers: investors, lenders, and other creditors deciding whether to commit capital to the entity.

Why this framing matters for an analyst: financial statements aren't designed purely for valuation — they're designed for the broader decision needs of resource providers. Useful as they are for valuation, that's a byproduct, not the primary design goal.

03

Relevance & Faithful Representation

Two fundamental qualities decide whether a number is even worth reporting at all — everything else is secondary.

The two fundamental qualitative characteristics

Relevance: information that could actually affect a user's decision — either by helping predict the future (predictive value) or by confirming/correcting a past judgment (confirmatory value).

Faithful representation: information that is complete, neutral, and free from error — it genuinely depicts the economic reality, without bias.

Materiality — relevance's practical filter

Information is material if leaving it out or getting it wrong could actually change a user's decision. Materiality is why a $500 rounding difference doesn't need its own disclosure at a company with $50 billion in revenue, but the exact same $500 might matter enormously at a small startup.

Worked example — the tension between the two

A company makes a credit sale and must simultaneously estimate an expense for potential bad debts it hasn't collected yet. Including that estimate makes the numbers more faithfully representative of the real economic period — but since the estimate isn't certain until later, it sacrifices some verifiability. This is a genuinely common trade-off: information can rarely satisfy every quality at once.

Check yourself
Information that is complete, neutral, and free from error is said to have which fundamental qualitative characteristic?
Correct — faithful representation specifically requires information to be complete, neutral, and free from error.

A useful memory hook: relevance asks "does this matter?" Faithful representation asks "is this actually true?" A number can be perfectly accurate and still irrelevant, or highly relevant and still misleading — you genuinely need both.

04

Enhancing Characteristics & Constraints

Four more qualities that make already-relevant, already-faithful information even more useful — plus the real-world limits on how far you can push all of this.

The four enhancing characteristics

Comparability: presented consistently enough, over time and across companies, that similarities and differences are actually visible.

Verifiability: independent, knowledgeable observers would agree the information faithfully represents what it claims to.

Timeliness: available to decision-makers before it becomes stale.

Understandability: clearly and concisely presented for users with reasonable business knowledge, willing to study it with diligence.

The pervasive constraint — cost

Producing and using financial information is never free. Ideally, the benefit of disclosing something exceeds the cost of gathering, verifying, and presenting it. This is exactly why financial statements don't try to capture absolutely everything — like a workforce's creativity or a strong corporate culture — even when those things genuinely drive value.

Check yourself
A company changes its accounting policy every year, making it hard to compare this year's results to prior years. Which enhancing characteristic is most directly compromised?
Correct — inconsistent presentation across periods directly undermines comparability, making it harder to identify genuine trends versus accounting changes.

The enhancing characteristics have no fixed priority order — sometimes timeliness wins out over perfect verifiability (an early estimate beats a late certainty), and the "right" balance depends on the specific situation.

05

The Elements of Financial Statements

Five formal categories. Every number in every financial statement belongs to exactly one of them.

Three elements measuring financial position

Assets: a present economic resource controlled by the entity, from a past event, with the potential to produce future economic benefit.

Liabilities: a present obligation to transfer an economic resource, from a past event, that the entity has no practical ability to avoid.

Equity: assets minus liabilities — the residual interest left for owners.

Two elements measuring performance

Income: increases in assets or decreases in liabilities that increase equity — other than contributions from owners. Includes both revenue (from ordinary operations) and gains (from other activities, like selling surplus equipment).

Expenses: decreases in assets or increases in liabilities that decrease equity — other than distributions to owners. Includes ordinary costs and losses (like an asset impairment).

Why "income" is a broader word than "revenue"

A software company's revenue comes from subscription sales — its ordinary business. If it also sells an old office building for more than its book value, that's a gain — still income, but not revenue, since selling buildings isn't what the company is actually in business to do.

Check yourself
A retailer sells surplus warehouse equipment it no longer needs, for more than the equipment's book value. How should this be classified?
Correct — this is a gain, since selling surplus equipment isn't the retailer's ordinary operating activity; it's still income, but distinct from revenue.

06

Underlying Assumptions — Accrual Accounting & Going Concern

Two quiet assumptions sit underneath every financial statement — change either one, and the numbers mean something completely different.

The two underlying assumptions

Accrual accounting: transactions are reflected when they actually occur, not necessarily when cash moves — already the core idea from Module 00.

Going concern: the company is assumed to continue operating into the foreseeable future — not about to be liquidated.

Why going concern actually changes the numbers

Inventory valued assuming a company can sell it over a normal sales cycle is worth one thing. The exact same inventory, valued assuming the whole company must liquidate everything within a week, is often worth considerably less — a forced, rushed sale rarely fetches full value. Whether going concern holds fundamentally changes how assets should be valued.

Check yourself
A company is on the verge of bankruptcy and expects to liquidate all its assets within the next month. Should its financial statements still assume going concern?
Correct — when liquidation is genuinely expected, the going concern assumption breaks down, and assets typically need to be valued on a different (often lower) basis.

Every ratio, every valuation model built on financial statements implicitly assumes going concern holds — it's rarely stated out loud, but it's foundational to nearly all standard analysis.

07

Bases of Measurement

"How much is this worth?" has at least six defensible answers — and the one a company picks changes the number that ends up on the page.

Six measurement bases

Historical cost: what was actually paid to acquire the asset.

Amortised cost: historical cost, adjusted for depreciation, amortization, or impairment since acquisition.

Current cost: what it would cost to buy the same or an equivalent asset today (replacement cost).

Realizable (settlement) value: what could be obtained by selling the asset today, in an orderly sale.

Present value: the discounted value of future cash flows the asset is expected to generate.

Fair value: the price that would be received to sell an asset (or paid to transfer a liability) in an orderly transaction between market participants.

Outside example — a delivery van, four different ways

A van bought 3 years ago for €30,000: historical cost is €30,000. Amortised cost is €30,000 minus three years of depreciation, say €18,000. Current cost is whatever an equivalent new van costs today, maybe €35,000 given inflation. Realizable value is whatever a buyer would actually pay for this specific, used van today, maybe €14,000. Four completely different, all individually defensible, numbers for the same physical object.

Check yourself
A company reports an asset at the amount it would cost to replace it with an equivalent new asset today. Which measurement basis is this?
Correct — current cost specifically refers to the cost of buying the same or an equivalent asset today, also called replacement cost.

Why this matters immediately ahead: Module 6 (Long-Lived Assets) revolves entirely around choosing between historical/amortised cost and fair value (the "revaluation model") — this section is the vocabulary that entire debate is conducted in.
